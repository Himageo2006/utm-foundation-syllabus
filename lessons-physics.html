Electrostatics

1.1 — Static electricity, charge & its conservation▶

Objects can be charged by rubbing. Charge comes in two types, positive and negative; like charges repel and opposite charges attract.

Law of conservation of electric charge: the net amount of electric charge produced in any process is zero — no net electric charge can be created or destroyed. The arithmetic sum of the total charge cannot change in any interaction.

Q = ne  ·  e = 1.61×10⁻¹⁹ C  ·  µ = ×10⁻⁶  n = ×10⁻⁹
Charge is quantized in units of e (charge on the electron e = 1.602×10⁻¹⁹ C).
1.1.1 — Insulators and conductors▶

Conductor: a material in which charge flows freely. The most common conductors are metals.

Insulator: a material in which almost no charge flows. Most non-metallic materials are insulators.

Two spheres connected by a metal nail (conductor) share charge from one to the other; connected by wood (insulator), almost no charge is conducted.
1.1.2 — Induced charge; the electroscope▶

Metal objects can be charged by conduction (charge by contact) and by induction (either while connected to ground or not).

  • A charged object brought near a neutral metal rod causes a separation of charge — the rod stays neutral overall.
  • Nonconductors won't become charged by conduction or induction, but will experience charge separation.

The electroscope (metal knob → rod → gold leaves, in a glass case) can be used for detecting charge, and a charged electroscope can determine the sign of an unknown charge.

1.2 — Coulomb's law▶

The electric force between two charges is proportional to the product of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. It acts along the line connecting them — attractive if opposite, repulsive if the same.

Fₑ = k|Q₁||Q₂| / r²  ·  k = 1/(4πε₀) = 9.00×10⁹ N·m²/C²  ·  ε₀ = 8.8542×10⁻¹² C²/N·m²
WORKED EXAMPLE 1 (from slides)

Two point charges, Q₁ = 85 µC and Q₂ = −50 µC, are separated by 3.5 cm. Find the magnitude and direction of (a) the force Q₁ exerts on Q₂, and (b) the force Q₂ exerts on Q₁.

1Q₁ = 85×10⁻⁶ C, Q₂ = 50×10⁻⁶ C, r = 3.5×10⁻² m.
2F = (9.0×10⁹)(85×10⁻⁶)(50×10⁻⁶)/(3.5×10⁻²)² = 3.12×10⁴ N
3(a) F on Q₂: to the left (towards Q₁). (b) F on Q₁: to the right (towards Q₂) — attractive, equal magnitude.
WORKED EXAMPLE 2 (from slides) — three charges in a line

Q₁ = −8.0 µC, Q₂ = +3.0 µC, Q₃ = −4.0 µC. Q₁→Q₂ = 0.30 m, Q₂→Q₃ = 0.20 m. Find the net force on Q₃.

1F₃₁ = (9×10⁹)(4×10⁻⁶)(8×10⁻⁶)/(0.5)² = 1.152 N (to right, +).
2F₃₂ = (9×10⁹)(4×10⁻⁶)(3×10⁻⁶)/(0.2)² = 2.7 N → −2.7 N (to left).
3Fₑ = −2.7 + 1.152 = −1.548 N (to the left)
WORKED EXAMPLE 3 (from slides) — net force in 2-D

Q₁ = −6.0 µC, Q₂ = +4.0 µC, Q₃ = −5.0 µC. r₁₂ = 20 cm (at 17° from vertical), r₂₃ = 12 cm (along x). Find the net force on Q₂.

1F₂₃ = (9×10⁹)(4×10⁻⁶)(5×10⁻⁶)/(0.12)² = 12.5 N (along +x).
2F₂₁ = (9×10⁹)(6×10⁻⁶)(4×10⁻⁶)/(0.20)² = 5.4 N (at 73° from +x).
3ΣFₓ = 12.5 + 5.4cos73° = 14.08 N; ΣF_y = 5.4sin73° = 5.16 N.
4|F₂| = √(14.08² + 5.16²) = 15.0 N at θ = 20.1° from +x axis.
Example 4 (slides): net force on Q₃ from Q₁, Q₂ — answer ≈ 290 N at 65° to the x axis (worked value 282.37 N at 61.74°).
1.3 — The electric field▶

The electric field strength at a point is the electric force per unit positive charge at that point, in the same direction as the force. It is a vector; units N·C⁻¹ or V·m⁻¹.

E = Fₑ/q₀  ·  E = kQ/r² = Q/(4πε₀r²)
Direction of E depends on the sign of the point charge only; direction of F depends on both the point charge and the test charge.
WORKED EXAMPLE 5 (from slides)

Q₁ = −3.0 µC and Q₂ = −5.0 µC are 12 cm and 30 cm from point P. Find (a) E at P, (b) force on q₀ = +1 µC at P, (c) distance from Q₁ where E = 0.

1E₁ₚ = (9×10⁹)(3.0×10⁻⁶)/(0.12)² = 1.88×10⁶ N/C (to left). E₂ₚ = (9×10⁹)(5.0×10⁻⁶)/(0.30)² = 5.00×10⁵ N/C (to right).
2Eₚ = −1.88×10⁶ + 5.00×10⁵ = −1.38×10⁶ N/C (to the left, towards Q₁).
3(b) Fₚ = q₀Eₚ = (1.0×10⁻⁶)(1.38×10⁶) = 1.38 N (to the left).
4(c) 3.0×10⁻⁶/x² = 5.0×10⁻⁶/(0.42−x)² → x = 0.183 m from Q₁.
WORKED EXAMPLE 6 (from slides)

Q₁ = −2.0 nC and Q₂ = +3.2 nC are 3.0 cm apart. Find the resultant E at point M (3-4-5 triangle, sin θ = 0.6, cos θ = 0.8).

1E₁ₘ = (9×10⁹)(2.0×10⁻⁹)/(0.05)² = 7.19×10³ N/C; E₂ₘ = (9×10⁹)(3.2×10⁻⁹)/(0.04)² = 1.80×10⁴ N/C.
2ΣEₓ = 1.80×10⁴ − 7.19×10³(0.8) = 1.23×10⁴ N/C; ΣE_y = 7.19×10³(0.6) = 4.31×10³ N/C.
3Eₘ = √((1.23×10⁴)² + (4.31×10³)²) = 1.30×10⁴ N/C at α = 19.3° from +x axis (anticlockwise).
Example 7 (slides): field above two charges — (a) E = 4.5×10⁶ N/C at 76° above x axis; (b) E = 3.6×10⁶ N/C along the x axis.
1.3.1 / 1.3.2 — Field lines & conductors▶

The electric field can be drawn as field lines that start on positive charges and end on negative charges. Characteristics:

  1. Lines indicate the field direction (tangent to the line at any point).
  2. Field magnitude ∝ number of lines per unit area — the closer the lines, the stronger the field.
  3. Lines start on + and end on −; their number ∝ magnitude of the charge.
  4. Field lines never cross (the field can't have two values at one point).

Electric fields and conductors: the static field inside a conductor is zero (else charges would move); net charge resides on the outer surface; and the field is perpendicular to the surface.

Between two oppositely charged parallel plates the field is uniform — parallel, equally spaced lines from + plate to − plate in the central region.
1.4 — Electric potential due to point charges▶

Electric potential V at a point is the potential energy per unit positive charge — or the work done bringing a positive test charge from infinity to that point per unit charge. It is a scalar; units Volt (V) or J·C⁻¹.

V = U/q₀ = W∞/q₀  ·  V = kQ/r = (1/4πε₀)(Q/r)  [V = 0 at r = ∞]
WORKED EXAMPLE 8 (from slides)

Q₁ = −40 µC, Q₂ = −30 µC, separated 15 cm. (a) Potential at A (5 cm from Q₁, 10 cm from Q₂); (b) potential at B (r₂ᵦ = 13 cm, r₁ᵦ = √(15²+13²) cm).

1Vₐ = 9×10⁹[(−40×10⁻⁶)/0.05 + (−30×10⁻⁶)/0.10] = −9.89×10⁶ V
2Meaning: 9.89×10⁶ J of work is done by the field to bring 1 C of + charge from infinity to A.
3r₁ᵦ = √(0.15²+0.13²) = 19.9×10⁻² m → Vᵦ = 9×10⁹[(−40×10⁻⁶)/0.199 + (−30×10⁻⁶)/0.13] = −3.88×10⁶ V
Exercise (slides): four charges Q, 2Q, −3Q, 2Q (Q = 4.8 µC) at corners of an 8.0 cm square → potential at centre = 1.53×10⁶ V (half-diagonal 0.0566 m).

1.4.1 Electric potential energy

The energy U of a charge q at a point where the potential is V:

U = qV = kQq/r  (show the sign of every charge)

1.4.2 Electric potential difference

Change in potential energy is the negative of the work done by the electric force:

V_ba = V_b − V_a = (U_b − U_a)/q = −W_ba/q  ·  W_ba = −q·ΔV_ba
WORKED EXAMPLE 10 (from slides)

Around a +5.4 nC charge, S is 6.0 cm away and T is 10 cm away (8.0 cm leg). (a) Find V_ST; (b) work to bring −1.5 nC from T to S.

1V_S = (9×10⁹)(5.4×10⁻⁹)/0.06 = 810 V; V_T = (9×10⁹)(5.4×10⁻⁹)/0.10 = 486 V.
2V_ST = 810 − 486 = 324 V
3W_TS = q₀V_TS = (−1.5×10⁻⁹)(324) = −4.86×10⁻⁷ J
WORKED EXAMPLE 11 (from slides) — electron in CRT

An electron is accelerated from rest through V_ba = +5000 V (m = 9.1×10⁻³¹ kg).

1ΔU = qV = (−1.6×10⁻¹⁹)(5000) = −8×10⁻¹⁶ J
2½mv² = −ΔU = 8×10⁻¹⁶ → v = 4.2×10⁷ m/s

1.4.3 Relation between potential and field

For a uniform field the simplest case is:

V_ba = −∫E·dℓ  →  V_ba = −Ed  [uniform E]

The negative sign means the potential decreases in the direction of the electric field.

WORKED EXAMPLE 12 (from slides)

Plates 5.0 mm apart, E = 1.0×10⁴ N/C, charge +4.0 nC moved across. (a) Work done; (b) potential difference.

1W = q₀Ed = (4.0×10⁻⁹)(1.0×10⁴)(5.0×10⁻³) = 2.0×10⁻⁷ J
2V = Ed = (1.0×10⁴)(5.0×10⁻³) = 50 V
WORKED EXAMPLE 13 (from slides)

Plates with V = 50 V, separation 0.050 m. Find E between the plates.

1V = Ed → 50 = E(0.05) → E = 1000 N·C⁻¹
Example 14 (slides) — charged conducting sphere of radius r₀, total charge Q: for r > r₀, V = (1/4πε₀)(Q/r); for r = r₀, V = (1/4πε₀)(Q/r₀); for r < r₀, V = constant = surface value (E = 0 inside, so V ∝ 1/r outside and flat inside).

Capacitor and Dielectric

2.1 — Capacitors and capacitance▶

A capacitor is a device capable of storing electric charge or electric potential energy — two conducting plates separated by a small air gap or a thin insulator (a dielectric such as mica, ceramics, paper or oil). Plates may be cylindrical, spherical or parallel.

Capacitance C is the ratio of the charge on either plate to the potential difference between them. It is a scalar, always positive; unit farad (F) = C·V⁻¹.

C = Q/V  →  Q = CV  (Q ∝ V)  ·  1 µF = 10⁻⁶ F, 1 pF = 10⁻¹² F

For a vacuum parallel-plate capacitor: E = Q/(Aε₀), V = dQ/(Aε₀), so

C₀ = ε₀A/d  ·  ε₀ = 8.85×10⁻¹² C²·N⁻¹·m⁻²
WORKED EXAMPLE 1 (from slides)

Plates of area 280 cm² separated 0.550 mm in vacuum, V = 20.1 V. Find (a) C, (b) charge per plate, (c) field strength.

1A = 280×10⁻⁴ m², d = 0.550×10⁻³ m.
2C = (8.85×10⁻¹²)(280×10⁻⁴)/(0.550×10⁻³) = 4.51×10⁻¹⁰ F
3Q = CV = (4.51×10⁻¹⁰)(20.1) = 9.07×10⁻⁹ C
4E = V/d = 20.1/(0.550×10⁻³) = 3.66×10⁴ V·m⁻¹
WORKED EXAMPLE 2 (from slides)

Plates 20 cm × 3.0 cm, 1.0-mm air gap, 12-V battery.

1(a) C = Aε₀/d = (6×10⁻³)(8.85×10⁻¹²)/(1×10⁻³) = 5.31×10⁻¹¹ F
2(b) Q = CV = (5.31×10⁻¹⁰)... = 6.372×10⁻⁹ C
3(c) E = V/d = 12/(1×10⁻³) = 1.2×10⁴ V/m
4(d) For C = 1 F with same gap: A = ≈ 1.13×10⁸ m²
2.2 — Capacitors in series and parallel▶

Series: same charge on each (Q = Q₁ = Q₂ = Q₃), voltages add (V = V₁+V₂+V₃):

1/C_eff = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ + 1/C₃ + … + 1/Cₙ

Parallel: same voltage on each (V = V₁ = V₂ = V₃), charges add (Q = Q₁+Q₂+Q₃):

C_eff = C₁ + C₂ + C₃ + … + Cₙ
WORKED EXAMPLE 3 (from slides)

Seven identical 2 µF capacitors in the network of Figure 5. Solve from the left end to the terminal.

1C₁,C₂,C₃ in series: 1/Cₓ = ½+½+½ → Cₓ = ⅔ µF
2Cₓ ∥ C₄: C_y = ⅔ + 2 = 8/3 µF
3C_y,C₅,C₆ in series: 1/C_z = 3/8+½+½ → C_z = 8/11 µF
4C_z ∥ C₇: C_eff = 8/11 + 2 = 30/11 µF ≈ 2.73 µF
WORKED EXAMPLE 4 (from slides)

C₁ = 100 µF, C₂ = 200 µF (parallel), in series with C₃ = 300 µF; V_AB = 8.0 V.

1C₁₂ = 100+200 = 300 µF; 1/C_eff = 1/300+1/300 → C_eff = 150 µF
2Q_T = C_eff·V_AB = (150µ)(8.0) = 1200 µC = Q₃ = Q₁₂.
3V₃ = 1200µ/300µ = 4.0 V; V₁₂ = 8.0−4.0 = 4.0 V = V₁ = V₂.
4Q₁ = C₁V₁ = (100µ)(4) = 400 µC; Q₂ = (200µ)(4) = 800 µC; V_AD = V₁₂ = 4.0 V.
Example 6 (slides): C = 3.0 µF, V = 4.0 V, C₂₃ = 2C = 6 µF parallel-then-series with C₁. Q_T = 8 µC; V₂₃ = 4/3 V; Q₂ = Q₃ = 4 µC; V₁ = 2.7 V.
2.3 — Electric energy storage▶

A charged capacitor stores energy equal to the work done to charge it:

U = ½Q²/C = ½CV² = ½QV

Energy density (energy per unit volume) of any electric field:

u = ½ε₀E²
Sudden discharge can be harmful or fatal — capacitors retain charge even when disconnected. Heart defibrillators use this discharge to restart the heart.
WORKED EXAMPLE 7 (from slides) — camera flash

A 150-µF capacitor at 200 V releases its energy in 1.0 ms.

1U = ½CV² = ½(150µ)(200²) = 3 J
2P = U/t = 3/(1×10⁻³) = 3000 W
2.4 — Dielectrics▶

A dielectric is a non-conducting material placed between the plates. Inserting it increases C by the dielectric constant ε_r (κ). Advantages: more capacitance, higher operating voltage, mechanical support (smaller d, larger C).

ε_r = C/C₀ = ε/ε₀ = V₀/V = E₀/E  (no unit)  ·  C = ε_r·ε₀A/d = Kε₀A/d

Dielectric strength = the field at which breakdown occurs and the material becomes a conductor. Battery connected → V constant, C and Q rise. Battery disconnected → Q constant, V drops.

WORKED EXAMPLE 8 (from slides)

Vacuum cap A = 150 cm², d = 2 mm, charged to V₀ = 2000 V, battery removed; with dielectric V drops to 500 V.

1(a) C₀ = ε₀A/d = (8.85×10⁻¹²)(150×10⁻⁴)/(2×10⁻³) = 6.63×10⁻¹¹ F
2(b) Q₀ = C₀V₀ = (6.63×10⁻¹¹)(2000) = 1.33×10⁻⁷ C
3(c) C = Q₀/V = 1.33×10⁻⁷/500 = 2.66×10⁻¹⁰ F
4(d) ε_r = C/C₀ = 4.01; (e) ε = ε_rε₀ = 3.55×10⁻¹¹ C²·N⁻¹·m⁻²
5(f) E₀ = V₀/d = 2000/(2×10⁻³) = 1.00×10⁶ V·m⁻¹; (g) E = E₀/ε_r = 2.49×10⁵ V·m⁻¹
WORKED EXAMPLE 9 (from slides) — dielectric removal

K = 3.4, 100-V battery then disconnected, A = 4.0 m², d = 4.0 mm.

1(a) C = Kε₀A/d = 3×10⁻⁸ F; Q = CV = 3×10⁻⁶ C; E = V/d = 2.5×10⁵ V/m; U = ½QV = 1.5×10⁻⁴ J
2(b) Dielectric removed (Q constant): C = ε₀A/d = 8.85×10⁻⁹ F; V = 3.4×100 = 340 V; E = 8.5×10⁴ N/C; U = ½QV = 5.1×10⁻⁴ J

Electric Current & Resistance

Ohm's law & power▶

Current I = Q/t (amperes). Ohm's law: V = IR. Power dissipated: P = VI = I²R = V²/R.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A 240 V supply drives 0.5 A through a heater. Find its resistance and power.

1R = V/I = 240/0.5 = 480 Ω
2P = VI = 240×0.5 = 120 W
4

DC Circuits

Series, parallel & Kirchhoff's laws▶
  • Series: R = R₁ + R₂ + … (same current).
  • Parallel: 1/R = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + … (same voltage).

Kirchhoff: (1) current in = current out at a junction; (2) sum of voltage drops round a loop = sum of EMFs.

WORKED EXAMPLE

Two resistors 6 Ω and 3 Ω in parallel, across 12 V. Find total resistance and total current.

11/R = 1/6 + 1/3 = 1/6 + 2/6 = 3/6 → R = 2 Ω
2I = V/R = 12/2 = 6 A

Magnetism

Magnetic force on charges & wires▶

A moving charge in a magnetic field feels a force F = qvB·sinθ. A current-carrying wire feels F = BIL·sinθ. Direction: Fleming's left-hand rule.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A wire of length 0.2 m carries 3 A perpendicular to a 0.5 T field. Find the force.

1θ = 90° so sinθ = 1.
2F = BIL = (0.5)(3)(0.2) = 0.3 N

Electromagnetic Induction

Faraday's & Lenz's laws▶

A changing magnetic flux Φ induces an EMF. Faraday: ε = −N·dΦ/dt. Lenz: the induced current opposes the change (that's the minus sign). Basis of generators & transformers.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A coil of 200 turns has its flux change by 0.01 Wb in 0.5 s. Find the induced EMF.

1ε = N·ΔΦ/Δt = 200 × (0.01/0.5)
2= 200 × 0.02 = 4 V

Electromagnetic Waves

The spectrum & wave equation▶

EM waves are oscillating electric & magnetic fields travelling at c = 3×10⁸ m/s in vacuum. The spectrum (long→short wavelength): radio, microwave, infrared, visible, ultraviolet, X-ray, gamma. Relation: c = fλ.

WORKED EXAMPLE

Find the frequency of green light, wavelength 5×10⁻⁷ m.

1f = c/λ = (3×10⁸)/(5×10⁻⁷)
2= 6×10¹⁴ Hz

Reflection & Refraction

Snell's law & total internal reflection▶

Reflection: angle in = angle out. Refraction (bending when changing medium): Snell's law n₁sinθ₁ = n₂sinθ₂. Refractive index n = c/v. Beyond the critical angle, light totally internally reflects.

WORKED EXAMPLE

Light goes from air (n=1) into glass (n=1.5) at 30°. Find the refraction angle.

1(1)sin30° = (1.5)sinθ₂ → 0.5 = 1.5 sinθ₂
2sinθ₂ = 0.333 → θ₂ = 19.5° (bends toward normal).

Lenses

Lens equation & magnification▶

Convex (converging) and concave (diverging) lenses form images. Lens equation: 1/f = 1/v − 1/u. Magnification m = v/u. (Use a consistent sign convention from your slides.)

WORKED EXAMPLE

An object is 30 cm from a convex lens of focal length 10 cm. Find the image distance.

11/f = 1/v + 1/u (real-is-positive form): 1/10 = 1/v + 1/30
21/v = 1/10 − 1/30 = 3/30 − 1/30 = 2/30
3v = 15 cm (real image).

Interference & Diffraction

Young's double slit & gratings▶

Light is a wave, so two coherent beams interfere: in phase → bright (constructive), out of phase → dark (destructive). Bright fringes satisfy d·sinθ = mλ.

WORKED EXAMPLE

A grating with slit spacing d = 2×10⁻⁶ m, light λ = 5×10⁻⁷ m. Find the angle of the 1st-order maximum.

1d·sinθ = mλ, m = 1: sinθ = λ/d = (5×10⁻⁷)/(2×10⁻⁶) = 0.25
2θ = 14.5°

Quantum Theory of the Atom

Photons & the photoelectric effect▶

Light energy comes in packets (photons) of energy E = hf, h = 6.63×10⁻³⁴ J·s. In the photoelectric effect, a photon ejects an electron if hf exceeds the work function W₀: hf = W₀ + ½mv²(max).

WORKED EXAMPLE

Find the energy of a photon of frequency 5×10¹⁴ Hz.

1E = hf = (6.63×10⁻³⁴)(5×10¹⁴)
2= 3.3×10⁻¹⁹ J (≈ 2.07 eV).

Nuclear Physics

Radioactivity & half-life▶

Unstable nuclei decay by emitting α (helium nucleus), β (electron), or γ (photon). Half-life T½ is the time for half the nuclei to decay: N = N₀·(½)^(t/T½). Mass defect → binding energy via E = mc².

WORKED EXAMPLE

A sample has 800 atoms; half-life is 5 days. How many remain after 15 days?

1Number of half-lives = 15/5 = 3.
2N = 800 × (½)³ = 800 × 1/8 = 100 atoms
13

Nuclear Reactions

Fission & fusion▶

Fission: a heavy nucleus splits into smaller ones, releasing energy (nuclear reactors). Fusion: light nuclei join (the Sun). Both release energy because the products have slightly less mass — that lost mass becomes energy via E = mc².

WORKED EXAMPLE

A reaction loses a mass of 3×10⁻²⁸ kg. How much energy is released?

1E = mc² = (3×10⁻²⁸)(3×10⁸)²
2= (3×10⁻²⁸)(9×10¹⁶) = 2.7×10⁻¹¹ J
