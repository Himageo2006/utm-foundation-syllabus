Thermochemistry

Thermochemistry is the study of energy (heat) changes during chemical reactions. These lessons follow the chapter slides exactly.

1.1 — Systems, surroundings & the First Law▶

The system is the part of the universe we study; the surroundings are everything else. Four types:

  • Open — exchanges mass and energy (open beaker).
  • Closed — exchanges energy only (coffee-cup calorimeter).
  • Insulated — no energy exchange (adiabatic).
  • Isolated — no mass or energy exchange (thermoflask).

First Law of Thermodynamics

Energy cannot be created or destroyed: E(universe) = E(system) + E(surroundings) = 0.

ΔE = q + w   (q = heat, w = work = PΔV)

Sign convention: +q heat enters the system; −q heat leaves. +w work done on the system; −w work done by the system.

1.2 — Enthalpy, exothermic & endothermic▶

Enthalpy (H) is the heat content at constant pressure. We measure the change, ΔH:

ΔH = ΔU + PΔV  ·  ΔH(reaction) = H(products) − H(reactants)

At constant pressure ΔH = qp; at constant volume ΔH = qv.

Two types

  • Exothermic — releases heat, surroundings warm up (Tfinal > Tinitial), ΔH negative. Heat is a product.
  • Endothermic — absorbs heat, surroundings cool, ΔH positive. Heat is a reactant.

Example (exothermic): H₂(g) + ½O₂(g) → H₂O(l), ΔH = −q kJ.

1.3 — Calorimetry: q = mcΔT (solution & bomb)▶

A calorimeter measures heats of reaction. Two types: the solution (coffee-cup) calorimeter (constant pressure) and the bomb calorimeter (constant volume, for combustion).

q = (m·s·ΔT) + (Cp·ΔT)  ·  ΔH = −( q / moles of limiting reactant )

m = mass of solution, s = specific heat, Cp = calorimeter heat capacity (often negligible → q = m·s·ΔT). For a bomb: q = C·ΔT.

WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides) — solution calorimeter

100 mL of 0.1 M CuSO₄ at 15.0 °C is mixed with excess zinc; T rises to 19.9 °C. (Cp=22.95 J/°C, s=4.2 J/g°C, ρ=1.0 g/mL.)

1q = [(100×1)×4.2×(19.9−15.0)] + [22.95×(19.9−15.0)] = 2170 J
2mol CuSO₄ = 0.100 L × 0.1 = 0.01 mol
3ΔH = −(2170/0.01) = −217 kJ/mol
4Zn(s) + CuSO₄(aq) → ZnSO₄(aq) + Cu(s), ΔH = −217 kJ/mol.
WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides) — bomb calorimeter

1.5 g CH₄ burned; ΔT = 7.3 °C; calorimeter constant C = 11.3 kJ/°C.

1q = C·ΔT = 11.3 × 7.3 = 83 kJ
2mol CH₄ = 1.5/16.04 = 0.094 mol
3ΔH = −(83/0.094) = −883 kJ/mol (exothermic). CH₄+2O₂→CO₂+2H₂O.
1.4 — Standard enthalpies (formation, combustion, neutralisation, solution)▶

Standard conditions: 1 atm, 1.0 M solutions, 298 K (25 °C), substances in standard states. The most stable allotrope has ΔH°f = 0 (e.g. graphite, O₂, H₂).

  • ΔH°f (formation) — heat change forming 1 mole of compound from its elements. e.g. H₂+½O₂→H₂O(l), ΔH°f = −286 kJ/mol.
  • ΔH°c (combustion) — heat when 1 mole is completely burned in excess O₂; always negative.
  • ΔH°n (neutralisation) — heat when 1 mol H⁺ + 1 mol OH⁻ → 1 mol H₂O. Strong acid+strong base ≈ −57.3 kJ/mol; weak acids/bases give less (energy used to dissociate them).
  • ΔH°soln (solution) — heat when 1 mol solute dissolves: ΔHsoln = (−ΔHlatt) + ΔHhyd,cation + ΔHhyd,anion.
WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides)

Enthalpy of solution of NaCl: ΔHhyd(Na⁺) = −390, ΔHhyd(Cl⁻) = −380, lattice energy = −776 kJ/mol.

1ΔHsoln = (+776) + (−390) + (−380) = +6 kJ/mol
1.5 — Hess's Law & Born–Haber cycle▶

Hess's Law: the total enthalpy change is independent of the route — add the steps. The equation form:

ΔHreaction = ΣΔH°f(products) − ΣΔH°f(reactants)
WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides)

Combustion of methane. ΔH°f: CH₄ = −74, CO₂ = −393, H₂O = −286 kJ/mol.

1CH₄ + 2O₂ → CO₂ + 2H₂O
2ΔHc = [(−393) + 2(−286)] − [(−74) + 0]
3= −965 + 74 = −891 kJ/mol

Born–Haber cycle

An energy cycle for ionic solids linking formation with lattice energy (which can't be measured directly): ΔHf(solid) = ΔHa + ΔHion + ΔHaffinity + ΔHlatt.

WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides) — NaCl

ΔHf=−411; Na: ΔHa=+108, IE=+500; Cl: ΔHa=+121, EA=−364 kJ. Find lattice energy.

1ΔHlatt = ΔHf − (ΔHa(Na)+ΔHa(Cl)+IE+EA)
2= −411 − (108+121+500−364) = −411 − 365 = −776 kJ/mol
⚛️ Also covered in the slides: enthalpy of atomization (ΔH°a, always +ve, breaking bonds) and bond energy, with ΔHf = ΔHa − ΔHbond.

Chemical Kinetics

Kinetics is the study of how fast reactions go — the change in concentration of a reactant or product with time (M/s). Built directly from the chapter slides.

2.1 — Rate & the differential rate equation▶
Rate = (change in concentration) / (time taken)   (always positive, M·s⁻¹)

Reactant concentration decreases (−); product increases (+). For aA + bB → cC + dD the rates link via the coefficients:

R = −(1/a)·d[A]/dt = −(1/b)·d[B]/dt = +(1/c)·d[C]/dt = +(1/d)·d[D]/dt
WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides)

For 2A + B → C + 3D, the reduction rate of A is 2.6×10⁻⁴ M/s. Find (a) the rate of reaction, (b) the rate of formation of D.

1R = −(1/2)·d[A]/dt = ½(2.6×10⁻⁴) = 1.3×10⁻⁴ M/s
2d[D]/dt = 3R = 3(1.3×10⁻⁴) = 3.9×10⁻⁴ M/s
2.2 — Rate law & order (from experimental data)▶
Rate = k[A]ˣ[B]ʸ   (k = rate constant; x, y = orders found by experiment; x+y = overall order)

Orders are found by experiment, not from stoichiometry — compare how rate changes when one concentration changes.

WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides) — 2NO + Br₂ → 2NOBr

Exp data: [NO]=0.1,[Br₂]=0.1→rate 12; [NO]=0.1,[Br₂]=0.2→24; [NO]=0.2,[Br₂]=0.1→48.

1Compare 1&2 ([Br₂] doubles): 24/12 = 2 = 2ʸ → y = 1.
2Compare 1&3 ([NO] doubles): 48/12 = 4 = 2ˣ → x = 2.
3Rate = k[NO]²[Br₂]; overall order = 3.
4Find k from Exp 1: 12 = k(0.1)²(0.1) → k = 1.2×10⁴ M⁻²s⁻¹
2.3 — Integrated rate laws & half-life (0, 1, 2 order)▶
OrderRate lawIntegratedHalf-life t½
0k[A]ₜ = −kt + [A]₀[A]₀/2k
1k[A]ln[A]ₜ = −kt + ln[A]₀0.693/k
2k[A]²1/[A]ₜ = kt + 1/[A]₀1/(k[A]₀)

Graphs (straight-line): 0 order → [A] vs t (slope −k); 1st → ln[A] vs t (slope −k); 2nd → 1/[A] vs t (slope +k).

WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides) — first order

2N₂O₅ → 4NO₂ + O₂, k = 5.1×10⁻⁴ s⁻¹, [A]₀ = 1.0×10⁻² M. Find [A] after 10 min.

1ln([A]ₜ/[A]₀) = −kt = −(5.1×10⁻⁴)(600 s)
2Solve: [A]ₜ = 7.36×10⁻³ M
Half-life behaviour: 0 order depends on [A]₀; 1st order t½ is constant (independent of concentration); 2nd order t½ is inversely proportional to [A]₀.
2.4 — The Arrhenius equation▶
k = A·e^(−Eₐ/RT)  or  ln k = ln A − Eₐ/RT  ·  ln(k₂/k₁) = (Eₐ/R)(1/T₁ − 1/T₂)

A = collision/frequency factor, Eₐ = activation energy, R = 8.314 J·mol⁻¹·K⁻¹, T in Kelvin. A plot of ln k vs 1/T is linear with slope = −Eₐ/R.

WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides)

k = 5.1×10⁻⁴ s⁻¹ at 318 K, Eₐ = 100 kJ/mol. Find k at 400 K.

1ln(k₂/k₁) = (−Eₐ/R)(1/T₂ − 1/T₁) = (−100000/8.314)(1/400 − 1/318)
2ln(k₂/k₁) = 7.75 → k₂/k₁ = e^7.75
3k₂ = 1.18 s⁻¹ at 400 K (much faster when hotter).
2.5 — Collision theory, activation energy & factors▶

Collision theory: molecules react only if they collide with (1) enough energy (≥ activation energy Eₐ) and (2) the correct orientation.

Activation energy (Eₐ) = minimum collision energy needed to break bonds and start the reaction. Lower Eₐ → faster reaction. The transition state (activated complex) is the unstable high-energy peak with partially broken/formed bonds.

Factors that increase rate

  • Concentration ↑ — more collisions.
  • Temperature ↑ — more frequent & more energetic collisions.
  • Surface area of solids ↑ — more contact.
  • Catalyst — lowers Eₐ.
2.6 — Reaction mechanism & rate-determining step▶

A mechanism is a series of elementary steps that sum to the overall equation. Key terms:

  • Rate-determining step — the slowest step; it sets the rate law.
  • Intermediate — appears in a step but not the overall equation.
  • Molecularity — number of molecules in a step (unimolecular, bimolecular, termolecular).
WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides)

Step 1: HI + HI → IH···HI (slow); Step 2: IH···HI → H₂ + 2I (fast); Step 3: 2I → I₂ (fast).

1Overall (sum): 2HI → H₂ + I₂.
2Intermediates: IH···HI and I.
3Rate law from slow step: Rate = k[HI]² → second order.
4Molecularity: step 1 bimolecular, step 2 unimolecular, step 3 bimolecular.
2.7 — Catalysts▶

A catalyst speeds up a reaction by lowering Eₐ (providing an alternative pathway) and is not consumed. Two types:

  • Homogeneous — same phase as reactants (e.g. Br₂ catalysing H₂O₂ decomposition; forms an intermediate).
  • Heterogeneous — different phase (e.g. platinum in a car catalytic converter: adsorption → reaction → desorption).

Chemical Equilibrium

Equilibrium applies to the extent of a reaction — how far it goes once no further change occurs. Built directly from the chapter slides.

3.1 — Dynamic equilibrium & reversibility▶

Irreversible reactions go in one direction almost exclusively (go to completion), e.g. 2Mg(s) + O₂(g) → 2MgO(s). Reversible reactions go both ways at comparable rates, e.g. CaCO₃(s) ⇌ CaO(s) + CO₂(g) and N₂(g) + 3H₂(g) ⇌ 2NH₃(g).

In a closed vessel the forward (k₁) and reverse (k₋₁) reactions continue at the same rate — the system looks "finished" but is dynamic:

At equilibrium: rateforward = ratereverse
On a concentration-vs-time graph, reactants fall and products rise until both level off — that plateau is equilibrium.
3.2 — The equilibrium constant expression (Kc & Kp)▶

For the equilibrium aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD:

K = ([C]ᶜ[D]ᵈ) / ([A]ᵃ[B]ᵇ)   (K depends only on temperature)

Examples from the slides:

2CO₂(g) ⇌ 2CO(g) + O₂(g):   K = [CO]²[O₂] / [CO₂]²
CH₄(g) + 2H₂S(g) ⇌ CS₂(g) + 4H₂(g):   Kc = [CS₂][H₂]⁴ / ([CH₄][H₂S]²)   or   Kp = (PCS₂)(PH₂)⁴ / ((PCH₄)(PH₂S)²)

Kc uses concentrations; Kp uses partial pressures.

3.3 — Calculating K from experimental data (ICE)▶
WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides) — H₂ + I₂ ⇌ 2HI

At 425°C, H₂ and I₂ each start at 5.73 atm. At equilibrium P(HI) = 9.00 atm. Find Kp.

1ICE: I 5.73 / 5.73 / 0; C −x / −x / +2x; E (5.73−x)/(5.73−x)/2x.
2P(HI) = 2x = 9.00 → x = 4.50 atm.
3P(H₂) = P(I₂) = 5.73 − 4.50 = 1.23 atm.
4Kp = (9.00)² / [(1.23)(1.23)] = 53.5
Products start at 0 — no product is formed yet at the start.
3.4 — Homogeneous vs heterogeneous equilibria▶

Homogeneous = all same state; heterogeneous = different states. Solids (s) and liquids (l) have constant concentration and are excluded from K. Only (g) and (aq) appear.

H₂(g) + FeO(s) ⇌ H₂O(g) + Fe(s):   Kc = [H₂O] / [H₂]
Ag⁺(aq) + Fe²⁺(aq) ⇌ Fe³⁺(aq) + Ag(s):   Kc = [Fe³⁺] / ([Ag⁺][Fe²⁺])
3.5 — What K tells you: position, direction & stoichiometry▶

Equilibrium position (10⁻²–10² range):

  • K = 1 (10⁻² < K < 10²) → conversion of reactants to products roughly equal.
  • K ≪ 1 (< 10⁻²) → conversion negligible; position lies to the left. e.g. COCl₂ ⇌ CO + Cl₂, Kc = 2.19×10⁻¹⁰.
  • K ≫ 1 (> 10²) → conversion high; position lies to the right. e.g. 2NO ⇌ N₂ + O₂, Kc = 1.00×10³⁰.

Stoichiometry & direction: if a reaction is reversed, K′ = K⁻¹; if multiplied by n, K′ = Kⁿ.

WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides) — reverse & multiply

2NO ⇌ N₂ + O₂ has Kc = 1.00×10³⁰.

1Reverse (N₂ + O₂ ⇌ 2NO): K′ = (10³⁰)⁻¹ = 1.00×10⁻³⁰
2For 4NO ⇌ 2N₂ + 2O₂ (×2): K = (10³⁰)² = 1.00×10⁶⁰
WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides) — 2HI ⇌ H₂ + I₂

1 L vessel at equilibrium: [HI]=1.75, [H₂]=1.125, [I₂]=0.125 M.

1Kc = [H₂][I₂]/[HI]² = (1.125)(0.125)/(1.75)² = 0.046
2Reverse: K′ = (0.046)⁻¹ = 21.7
3Forward Kc < 1 → position to the left, low formation of products.
WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides) — SO₂/O₂/SO₃ Kp

At equilibrium PSO₂=0.05, PO₂=0.03, PSO₃=1.00 atm.

1(a) 2SO₂+O₂ ⇌ 2SO₃: Kp = (1.00)²/[(0.05)²(0.03)] = 1.3×10⁴ atm⁻¹
2(b) SO₂+½O₂ ⇌ SO₃ is ½ of (a): K′p = (1.3×10⁴)^½ = 1.14×10² atm⁻¹
3.6 — Relationship between Kc and Kp▶
Kp = Kc(RT)Δn   Δn = (mol gas products) − (mol gas reactants), R = 0.0821 dm³·atm·K⁻¹·mol⁻¹, T in K

From PV = nRT → Px = MxRT = [x]RT, so substituting concentrations into Kp gives the (RT)Δn factor.

WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides) — at 480°C

2Cl₂(g) + 2H₂O(g) ⇌ 4HCl(g) + O₂(g), Kp = 0.0752. Find Kc.

1Δn = (4+1) − (2+2) = +1.
2Kc = Kp / (RT)Δn = 0.0752 / [(0.0821)(273+480)]¹
3= 1.22×10⁻³
3.7 — Predicting direction: the reaction quotient Q▶

Q has the same form as K but uses initial concentrations. Compare Q with Kc:

  • Q > Kc → too much product → shifts left.
  • Q < Kc → too much reactant → shifts right.
  • Q = Kc → at equilibrium, no shift.
WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides) — COCl₂ ⇌ CO + Cl₂

Kc = 2.19×10⁻¹⁰. Initial: [COCl₂]=3.50×10⁻³, [CO]=1.10×10⁻⁵, [Cl₂]=3.25×10⁻⁶ M.

1Q = (1.10×10⁻⁵)(3.25×10⁻⁶)/(3.50×10⁻³) = 1.02×10⁻⁸
2Q > Kc → too much product → shifts left.
3.8 — Calculating equilibrium concentrations▶
WORKED EXAMPLE 1 (from slides) — Br₂ + Cl₂ ⇌ 2BrCl

Kc = 7.0 at 400K. Start 0.50 M Br₂ and 0.50 M Cl₂ in 1 L.

1Q = 0/(0.5·0.5) = 0 < Kc → shifts right.
2ICE: E (0.50−x)/(0.50−x)/2x. 7.0 = (2x)²/(0.50−x)² → 2x/(0.50−x) = √7.
3x = 0.285 M → [BrCl] = 2x = 0.570 M; [Br₂]=[Cl₂]= 0.215 M
WORKED EXAMPLE 2 (from slides) — 2HBr ⇌ H₂ + Br₂

Kc = 6.25×10⁻⁶ at 1024K. Start 1.00 M HBr.

1ICE: E (1.00−2x)/x/x. 6.25×10⁻⁶ = x²/(1.00−2x)².
2Quadratic (or approximation, since K≪10⁻²) → x = 2.5×10⁻³.
3[H₂]=[Br₂]= 0.0025 M; [HBr] = 1.00−0.005 = 0.995 M
Approximation: when K ≪ 10⁻², the change x is tiny vs the initial concentration, so (1.00 − 2x) ≈ 1.00 — solve x = √(K·[A]₀²) directly.
3.9 — Le Chatelier's principle & the four factors▶

Le Chatelier: a system at equilibrium that is stressed shifts to counteract the disturbance. Four factors: concentration, pressure, temperature, catalyst. Slides use 6CO₂(g) + 6H₂O(g) ⇌ C₆H₁₂O₆(s) + 6O₂(g), ΔH = +2816 kJ.

  • Concentration: add reactant (↑[CO₂]) → shifts right; add product (↑[O₂]) → shifts left; remove a solid/liquid → no shift.
  • Pressure (↑ by ↓volume): shifts to the side with fewer gas moles. Adding inert gas at constant volume → no shift (partial pressures unchanged). ↑volume → shifts to side with more gas moles.
  • Temperature: endothermic (heat is a reactant) → ↑T shifts right and ↑K; exothermic (e.g. N₂+3H₂⇌2NH₃, ΔH=−42.6 kJ) → ↑T shifts left and ↓K.
  • Catalyst: lowers Eₐ for both directions equally → no shift; reaches equilibrium faster (changes rate & k, not the position).
StressShiftResult
[reactant] ↑right[product] ↑
[reactant] ↓left[product] ↓
[product] ↑left[reactant] ↑
[product] ↓right[reactant] ↓
3.10 — Solubility equilibrium & Ksp▶

Equilibrium between a slightly soluble solid and its dissolved ions. Solubility = g/L; molar solubility = mol/L. For MaXb(s) ⇌ aMb+ + bXa−:

Ksp = [Mb+]ᵃ[Xa−]ᵇ

Examples: AgCl → Ksp=[Ag⁺][Cl⁻]=1.6×10⁻¹⁰; Ca(OH)₂ → [Ca²⁺][OH⁻]²=6.5×10⁻⁶; Fe(OH)₃ → [Fe³⁺][OH⁻]³=1.1×10⁻³⁶.

WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides) — PbCl₂

Ksp(PbCl₂) = 1.7×10⁻⁴. Find molar solubility & solubility (MW = 278.2).

1PbCl₂ ⇌ Pb²⁺ + 2Cl⁻ → Ksp = (x)(2x)² = 4x³ = 1.7×10⁻⁴.
2x = ∛(1.7×10⁻⁴/4) = 3.5×10⁻² mol/dm³
3Solubility = 3.5×10⁻² × 278.2 = 9.74 g/dm³
WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides) — Ag₂S

Solubility 1.24×10⁻¹⁴ g/L (MW = 248). Find Ksp.

1Molar solubility x = 1.24×10⁻¹⁴/248 = 5.00×10⁻¹⁷ mol/L.
2Ag₂S ⇌ 2Ag⁺ + S²⁻ → Ksp = (2x)²(x) = 5.00×10⁻⁴⁹ M³
3.11 — The common ion effect▶

Common ions are ions from an external source that match ions already in equilibrium. Adding them causes a decrease in solubility and an increase in precipitation (equilibrium shifts left).

e.g. Ag₂SO₄(s) ⇌ 2Ag⁺ + SO₄²⁻ — adding AgNO₃ (extra Ag⁺) → too much product → shifts left → Ag₂SO₄ precipitates.

WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides) — will PbSO₄ precipitate?

Mix 0.10 L of 0.003 M Pb(NO₃)₂ with 0.40 L of 0.005 M Na₂SO₄. Ksp(PbSO₄) = 1.6×10⁻⁸.

1mol Pb²⁺ = 3×10⁻⁴; mol SO₄²⁻ = 2×10⁻³; total V = 0.50 L.
2[Pb²⁺]₀ = 6×10⁻⁴ M; [SO₄²⁻]₀ = 4×10⁻³ M.
3Q = (6×10⁻⁴)(4×10⁻³) = 2.4×10⁻⁶.
4Q > Ksp → shifts left → yes, PbSO₄ precipitates.

Acids & Bases

Acid–base equilibrium is the equilibrium of ions in aqueous solution (water as solvent). Built directly from the chapter slides.

4.1 — Three definitions: Arrhenius, Brønsted–Lowry, Lewis▶
  • Arrhenius: acid generates H⁺ (HCl → H⁺ + Cl⁻); base generates OH⁻ (NaOH → Na⁺ + OH⁻). Limited to H/OH compounds in water only — can't explain NH₃ or CO₂.
  • Brønsted–Lowry: acid donates a proton; base accepts one. Works for reversible reactions and forms conjugate pairs differing by one H⁺.
  • Lewis (widest): acid = electron-pair acceptor (electrophile, e.g. BF₃, Al³⁺); base = electron-pair donor (nucleophile, e.g. NH₃, OH⁻).

Classifying acids by protons: monoprotic (HCl→H⁺), diprotic (H₂SO₄→2H⁺), triprotic (H₃PO₄→3H⁺).

EXAMPLE (from slides) — conjugate pairs

NH₄⁺ + H₂O ⇌ NH₃ + H₃O⁺: NH₄⁺ (acid) → NH₃ (conj. base); H₂O (base) → H₃O⁺ (conj. acid).

1HF + H₂O ⇌ F⁻ + H₃O⁺ — pairs: HF/F⁻ and H₂O/H₃O⁺.
2Water is amphoteric — base with HCl, acid with NH₃.
4.2 — Kw, pH & the pH scale▶

Autoionization of water: 2H₂O ⇌ H₃O⁺ + OH⁻. Since [H₂O] is constant:

Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻] = 1.0×10⁻¹⁴ at 25°C  ·  pH = −log[H⁺]  ·  pOH = −log[OH⁻]  ·  pH + pOH = 14

Acidic: [H₃O⁺] > [OH⁻], pH < 7. Neutral: pH = 7. Basic: [H₃O⁺] < [OH⁻], pH > 7.

4.3 — Strength: strong vs weak; Ka, Kb & % ionization▶

Strong acids/bases ionize 100% (single arrow). 7 strong acids: HCl, HBr, HI, H₂SO₄, HNO₃, HClO₃, HClO₄. 7 strong bases: LiOH, NaOH, KOH, RbOH, Ca(OH)₂, Sr(OH)₂, Ba(OH)₂.

Weak acids/bases ionize partially (double arrow), measured by Ka / Kb. Larger K = stronger. pKa = −log Ka. ICE charts are used for weak acids/bases because they have a "C" change; strong ones ionize completely (no change in molarity).

% ionization = (moles ionized per litre / moles available per litre) × 100
Ka × Kb = Kw = 1.0×10⁻¹⁴  ·  pKa + pKb = 14 (acid & its conjugate base)
4.4 — pH of strong acids & bases▶
WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides)

(a) pH of 0.0750 M HI; (b) pOH & pH of 0.055 M Ca(OH)₂.

1HI → H⁺ + I⁻ (100%): [H⁺] = 0.0750 → pH = −log(0.0750) = 1.13
2Ca(OH)₂ → Ca²⁺ + 2OH⁻: [OH⁻] = 2(0.055). pOH = −log(0.110) = 0.96
3pH = 14 − 0.96 = 13.04
4.5 — pH of weak acids & bases (Ka / Kb)▶

Shortcut when K ≪ 10⁻²: [H⁺] = √(Ka·[HA]); [OH⁻] = √(Kb·[B]).

WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides) — weak acid HF

0.100 M HF, Ka = 6.8×10⁻⁴. Find pH and % ionization.

1Ka = x²/(0.100−x) ≈ x²/0.100 (since Ka ≪ 10⁻²).
2x = [H⁺] = √(6.8×10⁻⁴ × 0.1) = 8.25×10⁻³ → pH = 2.08
3% ionization = (8.25×10⁻³/0.100)×100 = 8.25%
WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides) — weak base hydrazine

0.0250 M N₂H₄, Kb = 1.7×10⁻⁶. (H₂O needed to generate OH⁻.)

1x = [OH⁻] = √(1.7×10⁻⁶ × 0.0250) = 2.06×10⁻⁴ M.
2[H⁺] = 1.0×10⁻¹⁴/2.06×10⁻⁴ = 4.85×10⁻¹¹ → pH = 10.31
4.6 — Salts & hydrolysis▶

A salt forms from acid + base; hydrolysis is the reaction of its ion with water. Resulting pH:

Acid + baseSaltpH
strong + strongNaClneutral (7)
weak acid + strong baseCH₃COONabasic (>7)
strong acid + weak baseNH₄Clacidic (<7)
weak + weakCH₃COONH₄depends on Ka/Kb

Anionic hydrolysis: A⁻ + H₂O ⇌ HA + OH⁻ (basic), Kh = Kw/Ka. Cationic: CH⁺ + H₂O ⇌ C + H₃O⁺ (acidic), Kh = Kw/Kb. For weak+weak: if Ka > Kb → acidic; Kb > Ka → basic; Ka ≈ Kb → neutral. % hydrolysis = ([ions] hydrolysed / [ions] initial)×100.

WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides) — 0.1 M NH₄Cl

Kb(NH₃) = 1.75×10⁻⁵. NH₄⁺ + H₂O ⇌ NH₃ + H₃O⁺.

1Kh = Kw/Kb = 1.0×10⁻¹⁴/1.75×10⁻⁵ = 5.7×10⁻¹⁰.
2x = [H₃O⁺] = √(5.7×10⁻¹⁰ × 0.10) = 7.5×10⁻⁶ → pH = 5.12 (acidic ✓)
WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides) — 0.05 M CH₃COONa

Kh = 5.6×10⁻¹⁰. CH₃COO⁻ + H₂O ⇌ CH₃COOH + OH⁻.

1x = [OH⁻] = √(5.6×10⁻¹⁰ × 0.05) = 5.29×10⁻⁶ → pOH = 5.28.
2pH = 14 − 5.28 = 8.72 (basic ✓)
4.7 — Buffer solutions & Henderson–Hasselbalch▶

A buffer resists pH change on adding small amounts of acid/base. It's a weak acid + its conjugate base (acidic buffer, e.g. CH₃COOH + CH₃COONa) or a weak base + its conjugate acid (basic buffer, e.g. NH₃ + NH₄Cl). Both must be present. HCl + NaCl is not a buffer (HCl is strong).

pH = pKa + log([base]/[acid])  ·  pOH = pKb + log([acid]/[base])

How it works: added H⁺ is absorbed by the conjugate base (CH₃COO⁻ + H⁺ → CH₃COOH); added OH⁻ is absorbed by the weak acid (CH₃COOH + OH⁻ → CH₃COO⁻ + H₂O).

WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides) — HF/NaF buffer

25 g NaF in 120 mL of 2.0 M HF, Ka(HF) = 6.8×10⁻⁴ (MW NaF = 42).

1mol F⁻ = 25/42 = 0.595; mol HF = 0.120×2.0 = 0.240.
2pKa = −log(6.8×10⁻⁴) = 3.17.
3pH = 3.17 + log(0.595/0.240) = 3.56
WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides) — buffer + acid/base

Same buffer, (a) add 2.50 mL of 2.0 M NaOH; (b) add 5 mL of 1.5 M HCl.

1(a) 0.005 mol OH⁻: HF 0.240→0.235, F⁻ 0.595→0.600 → pH = 3.17 + log(0.600/0.235) = 3.57 (+0.01)
2(b) 7.5×10⁻³ mol H⁺: F⁻ 0.595→0.588, HF 0.240→0.248 → pH = 3.17 + log(0.588/0.248) = 3.54 (−0.02)
4.8 — Acid–base titration & titration curves▶

Titration neutralizes an acid/base with a known standard to find concentration. Equivalence point: moles acid = moles base; located by an indicator (weak acid that changes colour over a pH range, e.g. phenolphthalein 8.2–10.0 colourless→pink) or a pH meter (steepest part of the curve).

  • Strong acid–strong base: equivalence pH = 7 — use any indicator pH 3.5–10.5.
  • Strong acid–weak base: equivalence pH < 7 (acidic) — use methyl orange (3.0–7.0).
  • Weak acid–strong base: equivalence pH > 7 (basic) — use phenolphthalein (6.5–10.5).
WORKED EXAMPLE (from slides) — weak acid + strong base equivalence

25.0 mL of 0.100 M acetic acid with 0.10 M NaOH, Ka = 1.8×10⁻⁵. Find pH at equivalence.

1mol acid = 0.025×0.100 = 2.5×10⁻³ → all converted to CH₃COO⁻ salt.
2[CH₃COO⁻] = 2.5×10⁻³/(0.025+0.025) = 0.05 M.
3Kh = Kw/Ka = 1.0×10⁻¹⁴/1.8×10⁻⁵ = 5.56×10⁻⁶.
4x = [OH⁻] = √(5.56×10⁻⁶ × 0.05) = 5.27×10⁻⁶ → pOH = 5.28 → pH = 8.72
📋 pH summary: strong → pH = −log[H⁺]; weak → use Ka/Kb; titration → MₐVₐ/M_bV_b = a/b; buffer → Henderson–Hasselbalch.
6

Organic Chemistry

Hydrocarbons, functional groups & naming▶

Organic chemistry = the chemistry of carbon compounds. Key families:

  • Alkanes (CₙH₂ₙ₊₂) — single bonds, e.g. methane CH₄.
  • Alkenes (CₙH₂ₙ) — one C=C double bond, e.g. ethene C₂H₄.
  • Alkynes (CₙH₂ₙ₋₂) — a C≡C triple bond.

Functional groups (–OH alcohol, –COOH carboxylic acid, etc.) define behaviour. Naming follows IUPAC: count the longest carbon chain, number it, name substituents.

WORKED EXAMPLE

Give the molecular formula of the alkane with 3 carbons (propane).

1Alkane formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ with n = 3.
2H = 2(3)+2 = 8 → C₃H₈
