Thermochemistry

Heat changes, enthalpy & Hess's law▶

Thermochemistry studies heat (energy) exchanged in reactions. Exothermic releases heat (ΔH negative); endothermic absorbs heat (ΔH positive).

q = m·c·ΔT  ·  ΔH(reaction) = ΣΔH(products) − ΣΔH(reactants)

Hess's law: total enthalpy change is the same whatever the route — so you can add reactions.

WORKED EXAMPLE

50 g of water (c = 4.18 J/g°C) rises by 20°C. Find the heat absorbed.

1q = m·c·ΔT = (50)(4.18)(20)
2= 4180 J = 4.18 kJ

Chemical Kinetics

Reaction rate & what affects it▶

Kinetics is about how fast reactions go. Rate increases with: higher concentration, higher temperature, larger surface area, and a catalyst (which lowers activation energy).

Rate law

Rate = k[A]ᵐ[B]ⁿ   (m, n = orders, found by experiment)

Collision theory: reactions happen when particles collide with enough energy (≥ activation energy) and correct orientation.

WORKED EXAMPLE

For Rate = k[A]², if [A] doubles, what happens to the rate?

1Rate ∝ [A]². Doubling A → factor 2² = 4.
2The rate becomes 4× faster.

Chemical Equilibrium

Kc & Le Chatelier's principle▶

Reversible reactions reach a dynamic balance where forward and reverse rates are equal. For aA + bB ⇌ cC + dD:

Kc = ([C]ᶜ[D]ᵈ) / ([A]ᵃ[B]ᵇ)

Le Chatelier: if you disturb a system at equilibrium, it shifts to oppose the change — add reactant → shifts right; increase pressure → shifts to fewer gas moles; increase temperature → shifts in the endothermic direction.

WORKED EXAMPLE

For N₂ + 3H₂ ⇌ 2NH₃, which way does increasing pressure shift it?

1Left side = 4 gas moles, right side = 2 gas moles.
2Higher pressure favours fewer moles → shifts right (more NH₃).

Acids & Bases

pH, strength & neutralisation▶

Acids release H⁺; bases release OH⁻ (Arrhenius) or accept H⁺ (Brønsted–Lowry). Acidity is measured by pH:

pH = −log[H⁺]  ·  Kw = [H⁺][OH⁻] = 1×10⁻¹⁴ at 25°C

Strong acids/bases ionise fully; weak ones partially (described by Ka / Kb). Acid + base → salt + water.

WORKED EXAMPLE

Find the pH of a solution with [H⁺] = 1×10⁻³ mol/dm³.

1pH = −log[H⁺] = −log(1×10⁻³)
2= −(−3) = 3 (acidic).

Organic Chemistry

Hydrocarbons, functional groups & naming▶

Organic chemistry = the chemistry of carbon compounds. Key families:

  • Alkanes (CₙH₂ₙ₊₂) — single bonds, e.g. methane CH₄.
  • Alkenes (CₙH₂ₙ) — one C=C double bond, e.g. ethene C₂H₄.
  • Alkynes (CₙH₂ₙ₋₂) — a C≡C triple bond.

Functional groups (–OH alcohol, –COOH carboxylic acid, etc.) define behaviour. Naming follows IUPAC: count the longest carbon chain, number it, name substituents.

WORKED EXAMPLE

Give the molecular formula of the alkane with 3 carbons (propane).

1Alkane formula CₙH₂ₙ₊₂ with n = 3.
2H = 2(3)+2 = 8 → C₃H₈
