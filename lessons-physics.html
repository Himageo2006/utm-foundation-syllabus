Electrostatics

1.1 — Static electricity, charge & its conservation▶
🟢 In plain words: Rub a balloon on your hair and it sticks to the wall — that's static electricity. Objects carry tiny amounts of charge, either positive or negative. The rule: opposites attract, sames repel. And charge is never created or destroyed — it only moves from one place to another.

Objects can be charged by rubbing. Charge comes in two types, positive and negative; like charges repel and opposite charges attract.

Law of conservation of electric charge: the net amount of electric charge produced in any process is zero — no net electric charge can be created or destroyed. The arithmetic sum of the total charge cannot change in any interaction.

Q = ne  ·  e = 1.61×10⁻¹⁹ C  ·  µ = ×10⁻⁶  n = ×10⁻⁹
Charge is quantized in units of e (charge on the electron e = 1.602×10⁻¹⁹ C).
1.1.1 — Insulators and conductors▶
🟢 In plain words: A conductor lets charge move through it easily (metals — think copper wires). An insulator blocks it (plastic, wood, rubber). That's literally why wires are metal inside and plastic outside.

Conductor: a material in which charge flows freely. The most common conductors are metals.

Insulator: a material in which almost no charge flows. Most non-metallic materials are insulators.

Two spheres connected by a metal nail (conductor) share charge from one to the other; connected by wood (insulator), almost no charge is conducted.
1.1.2 — Induced charge; the electroscope▶
🟢 In plain words: You can charge something without even touching it: bring a charged object close and it pushes/pulls the charges already inside, making one end positive and the other negative ("induced" charge). An electroscope is a simple gadget whose metal leaves spread apart when charged — a charge detector.

Metal objects can be charged by conduction (charge by contact) and by induction (either while connected to ground or not).

  • A charged object brought near a neutral metal rod causes a separation of charge — the rod stays neutral overall.
  • Nonconductors won't become charged by conduction or induction, but will experience charge separation.

The electroscope (metal knob → rod → gold leaves, in a glass case) can be used for detecting charge, and a charged electroscope can determine the sign of an unknown charge.

1.2 — Coulomb's law▶
🟢 In plain words: Coulomb's law is the formula for how strongly two charges push or pull each other. Bigger charges → stronger force; further apart → much weaker force (it drops with distance squared, so double the distance = quarter the force). It's basically gravity's electric cousin.

The electric force between two charges is proportional to the product of the charges and inversely proportional to the square of the distance between them. It acts along the line connecting them — attractive if opposite, repulsive if the same.

Fₑ = k|Q₁||Q₂| / r²  ·  k = 1/(4πε₀) = 9.00×10⁹ N·m²/C²  ·  ε₀ = 8.8542×10⁻¹² C²/N·m²

1.3 — The electric field▶
🟢 In plain words: A charge creates an invisible "zone of influence" around it called an electric field — any other charge placed in that zone feels a force. The field strength E tells you how much force per unit charge at each point. Think of it as the charge's "reach."

The electric field strength at a point is the electric force per unit positive charge at that point, in the same direction as the force. It is a vector; units N·C⁻¹ or V·m⁻¹.

E = Fₑ/q₀  ·  E = kQ/r² = Q/(4πε₀r²)
Direction of E depends on the sign of the point charge only; direction of F depends on both the point charge and the test charge.

4(c) 3.0×10⁻⁶/x² = 5.0×10⁻⁶/(0.42−x)² → x = 0.183 m from Q₁.

1.3.1 / 1.3.2 — Field lines & conductors▶
🟢 In plain words: We draw electric fields as arrows/lines flowing from + charges to − charges. Where the lines bunch up close together, the field is strong. Two handy facts: the lines never cross, and inside a metal conductor the field is zero (charges sit on the outside surface).

The electric field can be drawn as field lines that start on positive charges and end on negative charges. Characteristics:

  1. Lines indicate the field direction (tangent to the line at any point).
  2. Field magnitude ∝ number of lines per unit area — the closer the lines, the stronger the field.
  3. Lines start on + and end on −; their number ∝ magnitude of the charge.
  4. Field lines never cross (the field can't have two values at one point).

Electric fields and conductors: the static field inside a conductor is zero (else charges would move); net charge resides on the outer surface; and the field is perpendicular to the surface.

Between two oppositely charged parallel plates the field is uniform — parallel, equally spaced lines from + plate to − plate in the central region.
1.4 — Electric potential due to point charges▶
🟢 In plain words: Electric potential (measured in volts) is like "electrical height" — how much energy a charge would have at that spot, per unit of charge. Just as a ball rolls from high ground to low, positive charges naturally move from high voltage to low. Unlike the field, potential is a plain number (no direction), so you just add them up.

Electric potential V at a point is the potential energy per unit positive charge — or the work done bringing a positive test charge from infinity to that point per unit charge. It is a scalar; units Volt (V) or J·C⁻¹.

V = U/q₀ = W∞/q₀  ·  V = kQ/r = (1/4πε₀)(Q/r)  [V = 0 at r = ∞]

1.4.1 Electric potential energy

The energy U of a charge q at a point where the potential is V:

U = qV = kQq/r  (show the sign of every charge)

1.4.2 Electric potential difference

Change in potential energy is the negative of the work done by the electric force:

V_ba = V_b − V_a = (U_b − U_a)/q = −W_ba/q  ·  W_ba = −q·ΔV_ba

3W_TS = q₀V_TS = (−1.5×10⁻⁹)(324) = −4.86×10⁻⁷ J

1.4.3 Relation between potential and field

For a uniform field the simplest case is:

V_ba = −∫E·dℓ  →  V_ba = −Ed  [uniform E]

The negative sign means the potential decreases in the direction of the electric field.

Capacitor and Dielectric

2.1 — Capacitors and capacitance▶
🟢 In plain words: A capacitor is a little energy tank — two metal plates that store electric charge, ready to release it in a burst (like a camera flash). "Capacitance" just measures how much charge it can hold per volt — a bigger number means a bigger tank.

A capacitor is a device capable of storing electric charge or electric potential energy — two conducting plates separated by a small air gap or a thin insulator (a dielectric such as mica, ceramics, paper or oil). Plates may be cylindrical, spherical or parallel.

Capacitance C is the ratio of the charge on either plate to the potential difference between them. It is a scalar, always positive; unit farad (F) = C·V⁻¹.

C = Q/V  →  Q = CV  (Q ∝ V)  ·  1 µF = 10⁻⁶ F, 1 pF = 10⁻¹² F

For a vacuum parallel-plate capacitor: E = Q/(Aε₀), V = dQ/(Aε₀), so

C₀ = ε₀A/d  ·  ε₀ = 8.85×10⁻¹² C²·N⁻¹·m⁻²
WORKED EXAMPLE 1 (from slides)

Plates of area 280 cm² separated 0.550 mm in vacuum, V = 20.1 V. Find (a) C, (b) charge per plate, (c) field strength.

1A = 280×10⁻⁴ m², d = 0.550×10⁻³ m.
2C = (8.85×10⁻¹²)(280×10⁻⁴)/(0.550×10⁻³) = 4.51×10⁻¹⁰ F
3Q = CV = (4.51×10⁻¹⁰)(20.1) = 9.07×10⁻⁹ C
4E = V/d = 20.1/(0.550×10⁻³) = 3.66×10⁴ V·m⁻¹
WORKED EXAMPLE 2 (from slides)

Plates 20 cm × 3.0 cm, 1.0-mm air gap, 12-V battery.

1(a) C = Aε₀/d = (6×10⁻³)(8.85×10⁻¹²)/(1×10⁻³) = 5.31×10⁻¹¹ F
2(b) Q = CV = (5.31×10⁻¹⁰)... = 6.372×10⁻⁹ C
3(c) E = V/d = 12/(1×10⁻³) = 1.2×10⁴ V/m
4(d) For C = 1 F with same gap: A = ≈ 1.13×10⁸ m²
2.2 — Capacitors in series and parallel▶
🟢 In plain words: When you connect several capacitors, the combining rules are the opposite of resistors. In parallel (side by side) you just add them up — more total storage. In series (in a line) you add the reciprocals, giving less than the smallest. Same charge in series, same voltage in parallel.

Series: same charge on each (Q = Q₁ = Q₂ = Q₃), voltages add (V = V₁+V₂+V₃):

1/C_eff = 1/C₁ + 1/C₂ + 1/C₃ + … + 1/Cₙ

Parallel: same voltage on each (V = V₁ = V₂ = V₃), charges add (Q = Q₁+Q₂+Q₃):

C_eff = C₁ + C₂ + C₃ + … + Cₙ

2.3 — Electric energy storage▶
🟢 In plain words: Charging a capacitor stores energy (you did work pushing charge onto it). Three equivalent formulas give that stored energy — pick whichever matches the info you have (Q, C, or V). That stored energy is what a defibrillator or camera flash dumps out all at once.

A charged capacitor stores energy equal to the work done to charge it:

U = ½Q²/C = ½CV² = ½QV

Energy density (energy per unit volume) of any electric field:

u = ½ε₀E²
Sudden discharge can be harmful or fatal — capacitors retain charge even when disconnected. Heart defibrillators use this discharge to restart the heart.
WORKED EXAMPLE 7 (from slides) — camera flash

A 150-µF capacitor at 200 V releases its energy in 1.0 ms.

1U = ½CV² = ½(150µ)(200²) = 3 J
2P = U/t = 3/(1×10⁻³) = 3000 W
2.4 — Dielectrics▶
🟢 In plain words: A dielectric is an insulating slab (plastic, mica, paper) slipped between the plates. It boosts the capacitance — letting the capacitor store more charge and handle higher voltage in a smaller size. That's why real capacitors aren't just two plates in air.

A dielectric is a non-conducting material placed between the plates. Inserting it increases C by the dielectric constant ε_r (κ). Advantages: more capacitance, higher operating voltage, mechanical support (smaller d, larger C).

ε_r = C/C₀ = ε/ε₀ = V₀/V = E₀/E  (no unit)  ·  C = ε_r·ε₀A/d = Kε₀A/d

Dielectric strength = the field at which breakdown occurs and the material becomes a conductor. Battery connected → V constant, C and Q rise. Battery disconnected → Q constant, V drops.

WORKED EXAMPLE 8 (from slides)

Vacuum cap A = 150 cm², d = 2 mm, charged to V₀ = 2000 V, battery removed; with dielectric V drops to 500 V.

1(a) C₀ = ε₀A/d = (8.85×10⁻¹²)(150×10⁻⁴)/(2×10⁻³) = 6.63×10⁻¹¹ F
2(b) Q₀ = C₀V₀ = (6.63×10⁻¹¹)(2000) = 1.33×10⁻⁷ C
3(c) C = Q₀/V = 1.33×10⁻⁷/500 = 2.66×10⁻¹⁰ F
4(d) ε_r = C/C₀ = 4.01; (e) ε = ε_rε₀ = 3.55×10⁻¹¹ C²·N⁻¹·m⁻²
5(f) E₀ = V₀/d = 2000/(2×10⁻³) = 1.00×10⁶ V·m⁻¹; (g) E = E₀/ε_r = 2.49×10⁵ V·m⁻¹
WORKED EXAMPLE 9 (from slides) — dielectric removal

K = 3.4, 100-V battery then disconnected, A = 4.0 m², d = 4.0 mm.

1(a) C = Kε₀A/d = 3×10⁻⁸ F; Q = CV = 3×10⁻⁶ C; E = V/d = 2.5×10⁵ V/m; U = ½QV = 1.5×10⁻⁴ J
2(b) Dielectric removed (Q constant): C = ε₀A/d = 8.85×10⁻⁹ F; V = 3.4×100 = 340 V; E = 8.5×10⁴ N/C; U = ½QV = 5.1×10⁻⁴ J

Electric Currents and Resistance

3.1 / 3.2 — The electric battery & electric current▶
🟢 In plain words: A battery turns chemical energy into electrical energy, pushing charge around a circuit. Current is just how much charge flows past a point each second (like water flow rate in a pipe), measured in amps. Heads-up on a historical quirk: "conventional current" is drawn flowing +→−, even though the electrons actually drift the other way.

Volta found electricity is created when dissimilar metals are joined by a conductive solution (an electrolyte) — a simple cell. A battery transforms chemical energy into electrical energy; chemical reactions maintain a potential difference between the terminals. Several cells together make a battery.

Electric current I is the total (net) charge Q flowing through an area per unit time. Base and scalar quantity; S.I. unit ampere (A) = 1 C·s⁻¹.

I = Q/t (average) · I = dQ/dt (instantaneous) · Q = ne, e = 1.6×10⁻¹⁹ C

Conventional current (and E field) flows positive → negative terminal; electron flow is negative → positive. A complete circuit lets current flow all the way round; an open circuit has a break (no current). Steady one-direction current is direct current (DC) from a battery.

WORKED EXAMPLE 1 (from slides) — current is flow of charge

A steady 2.5 A flows in a wire for 4.0 min. (a) Total charge? (b) How many electrons?

1t = 4×60 = 240 s; Q = It = 2.5(240) = 600 C
2n = Q/e = 600/(1.6×10⁻¹⁹) = 3.75×10²¹ electrons
3.3 — Ohm's law: resistance and resistors▶
🟢 In plain words: Resistance is how much a component "fights" the current. Ohm's law ties the three big quantities together in one tidy triangle: V = I × R (voltage = current × resistance). Push harder (more volts) → more current; more resistance → less current.

Ohm's law: the potential difference across a metallic conductor is proportional to the current through it, at constant temperature.

V ∝ I → V = IR · R = V/I (unit ohm Ω = V·A⁻¹)

Materials obeying Ohm's law are ohmic (e.g. pure metal, constantan) — the I–V graph is a straight line through the origin (gradient 1/R). Materials that don't are non-ohmic. Resistance measures opposition to charge flow; it is a scalar, and stays constant if temperature is constant. Standard resistors are colour-coded for value and tolerance.

WORKED EXAMPLE 2 (from slides) — flashlight bulb

A bulb draws 300 mA from a 1.5-V battery. (a) Resistance? (b) Current if V drops to 1.2 V?

1(a) R = V/I = 1.5/(300×10⁻³) = 5 Ω
2(b) I = V/R = 1.2/5 = 0.24 A — the current decreases.
3.4 — Resistivity▶
🟢 In plain words: Resistance depends on the wire's shape, but resistivity (ρ) is a property of the material itself — copper has low resistivity (good conductor), rubber has huge resistivity (insulator). A wire's resistance = resistivity × length ÷ thickness: long & thin = high resistance; short & fat = low.

Resistivity ρ is the resistance of unit cross-sectional area per unit length. Scalar; unit Ω·m. It measures a material's ability to oppose current and depends on the material and temperature — good conductors have very low ρ, good insulators very high ρ.

ρ = RA/ℓ → R = ρℓ/A · ρ_T = ρ₀[1 + α(T − T₀)]

3.5 — Electric power; power in the household▶
🟢 In plain words: Power is how fast electrical energy is used, in watts. The everyday formula is P = V × I (and you can rewrite it as I²R or V²/R). This is what your electricity bill charges for — energy = power × time, measured in kilowatt-hours (kWh).

Power is the energy liberated per unit time. Scalar; unit watt (W).

P = W/t = VI · P = I²R = V²/R · 1 kWh = (1000 W)(3600 s) = 3.60×10⁶ J

2Q = It → 20 = I(0.2) → I = 100 A
3P = VI = (5×10⁷)(100) = 5×10⁹ W
Household wires have low resistance, but high current can heat them enough to start a fire — fuses/circuit breakers disconnect above a set current.
3.6 — Alternating current▶
🟢 In plain words: Batteries give steady DC (one direction). Wall sockets give AC — the current rapidly flips back and forth. Since it averages to zero, we describe its "effective" size with the rms value (≈ 0.707 of the peak), which is what gives the same heating as an equivalent DC.

For AC, I = I₀sin ωt and power P = I²R = I₀²R sin²ωt. Since current and voltage average zero, we use root-mean-square (rms) values:

I_rms = I₀/√2 = 0.707 I₀ · V_rms = V₀/√2 = 0.707 V₀
P̄ = ½I₀²R = ½V₀²/R = ½I₀V₀ · P(AC) = V_rms·I_rms · V_rms = I_rms·R
WORKED EXAMPLE 9 (from slides) — hair dryer

1000-W hair dryer on 120 V. (a) Resistance and peak current? (b) On a 240-V line?

1P = VI → 1000 = 120·I_rms → I_rms = 8.33 A; I₀ = I_rms√2 = 11.78 A (peak).
2V_rms = I_rms·R → 120 = 8.33R → R = 14.4 Ω
3(b) On 240 V: P = V_rms²/R = 240²/14.4 = 4000 W (overheats).

DC Circuits

4.1 — EMF and terminal voltage▶
🟢 In plain words: A battery's "emf" is its full advertised voltage (e.g. 9 V). But the battery also has a little resistance inside it, so when current flows, some voltage is lost internally and the terminal voltage you actually get is a bit less: V = ε − Ir. That's why a battery sags under heavy load.

Electromotive force (emf) ε is the energy provided by the source (battery/cell) to each unit charge passing through the external and internal resistances. Terminal voltage V is the work done bringing unit charge from − to + terminal through the external resistance only. Both are in volts.

When current I flows there is an internal drop Ir, so:

V = ε − Ir · V = IR · ε = I(R + r)  (when no current, V = ε)

1(a) ε = V + Ir → 9.0 = V + (0.50)(6.0) → V = 6.0 V
2(b) Max current at R = 0: 9.0 = I_max(0 + 6.0) → I_max = 1.5 A

1ε = I(R + r) → 12 = I(65.5) → I = 0.183 A

3P_R = I²R = 0.183²(65) = 2.18 W; P_r = I²r = 0.183²(0.5) = 0.017 W
4.2 — Resistors in series and in parallel▶
🟢 In plain words: Series (one after another) → just add the resistances; the same current flows through all. Parallel (side by side) → add the reciprocals, giving less than the smallest, because the current now has multiple paths. For tricky networks, simplify a small chunk at a time.

Series (same current I = I₁ = I₂ = I₃; voltages add V = V₁+V₂+V₃):

R_eff = R₁ + R₂ + R₃

Parallel (same voltage V = V₁ = V₂ = V₃; currents add I = I₁+I₂+I₃):

1/R_eff = 1/R₁ + 1/R₂ + 1/R₃

4.3 — Kirchhoff's rules▶
🟢 In plain words: For circuits too complex for simple series/parallel, two common-sense rules crack them: (1) Junction rule — whatever current flows into a junction must flow out (nothing piles up). (2) Loop rule — go around any loop and all the voltage gains and drops must cancel to zero (energy is conserved). Set up the equations, then solve.

First law (junction/current): ΣI_in = ΣI_out — charge is conserved at a junction.

Second law (loop/voltage): Σε = ΣIR — round any closed loop the sum of emfs equals the sum of IR products.

Sign convention — emf: + when loop goes − → +; IR: + when loop direction follows the current. A negative current means its real direction is opposite to that drawn.

4.4 — RC circuits (resistor + capacitor)▶
🟢 In plain words: Put a resistor and capacitor together and the capacitor charges up (or drains) gradually, not instantly — fast at first, then slowing down (an exponential curve). The "time constant" τ = R×C tells you the speed: after one τ it's about 63% charged. This is the timing trick behind flashing lights and timers.

Closing the switch charges the capacitor: voltage rises, current falls. Around the loop ε = IR + Q/C.

Charging:

Q = Q₀(1 − e^(−t/RC)) · V = V₀(1 − e^(−t/RC)) · I = I₀e^(−t/RC), I₀ = V₀/R

Discharging:

Q = Q₀e^(−t/RC) · V = V₀e^(−t/RC) · I = −I₀e^(−t/RC), I₀ = Q₀/RC

Time constant τ = RC (seconds): time to reach 63% of max charge when charging, or fall to 37% of initial value when discharging.

Magnetism

5.1 — Magnets and Magnetic Fields▶
🟢 In plain words: Magnets have a north and south pole — likes repel, opposites attract (just like charges). A magnetic field is the region around a magnet where this force is felt, drawn as lines from N to S. Fun fact: you can never get a lone pole — snap a magnet in two and each piece grows its own N and S.

Magnets have two ends — poles — called north (N) and south (S). Like poles repel; unlike poles attract.

If you cut a magnet in half, you don't get a separate north pole and south pole — you get two smaller magnets (each with both N and S). A single magnetic pole has never been found; poles always come in pairs.

The Earth's magnetic field is similar to that of a bar magnet. Note that the Earth's geographic "North Pole" is really a south magnetic pole, since the north ends of magnets are attracted to it.

Magnetic field is defined as a region around a magnet where a magnetic force can be experienced.

  • A stationary electric charge is surrounded by an electric field only.
  • When an electric charge moves, it is surrounded by both an electric field and a magnetic field — the motion of the charge produces the magnetic field.

Magnetic field lines represent a magnetic field. By convention, field lines leave the north pole and enter the south pole. The tangent to a curved field line at a point gives the direction of the field at that point. The pattern can be shown using compass needles or by sprinkling iron filings on paper.

A neutral point is a point where the resultant magnetic force is zero (e.g. between two like poles).
5.2 — Ampère's Law, definition of the Ampere▶
🟢 In plain words: Electric currents make magnetic fields. Ampère's law is a shortcut for working out that field around a current: add up the field around a closed loop and it equals μ₀ × the current passing through. For a straight wire it neatly gives B = μ₀I/2πr (stronger nearer the wire).

Ampère's law relates the magnetic field around a closed loop to the total current flowing through the loop:

∮ B·dℓ = μ₀ I_encl

The integral is taken around the edge of the closed loop. For a long straight wire, use a circular path with the wire at the centre; then B is tangent to dℓ at every point and μ₀I = ∮B·dℓ = B(2πr), giving B = μ₀I/2πr.

Definition of the ampere: one ampere is the current flowing in each of two long parallel wires 1 m apart that results in a force of exactly 2×10⁻⁷ N per metre of length. The coulomb is then defined as exactly one ampere-second.
WORKED EXAMPLE 1 (from slides)

Field inside and outside a wire. A long straight cylindrical conductor of radius R carries a current I of uniform current density. With R = 2.0 mm and I = 60 A, find B at r = 1.0 mm, 2.0 mm and 3.0 mm.

1Outside (r > R): B = μ₀I/2πr. Inside (r < R): B = μ₀Ir/2πR² (since I_encl = I·πr²/πR²).
2r = 1.0 mm (inside): B = (4π×10⁻⁷·60·1×10⁻³)/(2π(2×10⁻³)²) = 6×10⁻⁶ T
3r = 2.0 mm (= R): B = μ₀I/2πr = (4π×10⁻⁷·60)/(2π·2×10⁻³) = 6×10⁻³ T
4r = 3.0 mm (outside): B = (4π×10⁻⁷·60)/(2π·3×10⁻³) = 4×10⁻³ T
5.3.1 — Magnetic field due to a straight wire▶
🟢 In plain words: A straight wire carrying current is wrapped in circular magnetic field loops. To find their direction, use the right-hand grip rule: point your thumb along the current and your curled fingers show which way the field circles. Strength: B = μ₀I/2πr — more current or closer = stronger.

An electric current produces a magnetic field. The direction is given by the right-hand grip rule: thumb points along the current, the curled fingers give the field direction (clockwise or anticlockwise circles around the wire).

Magnitude of the magnetic flux density at distance r from a long straight wire:

B = μ₀I / 2πr   (μ₀ = 4π×10⁻⁷ T·m·A⁻¹)

5.3.2 — Magnetic field of a solenoid▶
🟢 In plain words: A solenoid is just a wire coiled into many loops. Stacking the loops adds their fields together, making a strong, uniform field down the middle — basically a magnet you can switch on and off (an electromagnet). The field only depends on the current and how tightly packed the turns are (n = turns per metre).

A solenoid is a long wire wound into a succession of closely spaced loops (a helix). The right-hand grip rule for a solenoid: fingers follow the current in the coils, thumb points to the north pole.

At the centre (inside) of an N-turn solenoid, with n = N/ℓ turns per unit length:

B = μ₀NI/ℓ = μ₀nI

At the end of the solenoid the field is half this value:

B = ½ μ₀nI

1n = N/ℓ = 400/0.1 = 4000 turns/m.

5.4.1 — Force on a current-carrying conductor▶
🟢 In plain words: Put a current-carrying wire into a magnetic field and the field pushes the wire (this is how electric motors spin). The push is strongest when the wire sits at right angles to the field (F = BIℓ) and zero when it lies along the field. Fleming's left-hand rule gives the push direction.

When a current-carrying conductor is placed in a magnetic field B, it experiences a magnetic force:

F = IℓB·sinθ  (vector: F = I ℓ × B)

θ is the angle between the current direction and B. The force is maximum (F = IℓB) when the conductor is perpendicular to B (θ = 90°), and zero when the conductor is parallel to B (θ = 0°).

Fleming's left-hand rule: Thumb = Force, First finger = Field, Second finger = Current.

5.4.2 — Force on a moving electric charge▶
🟢 In plain words: A single moving charge feels the same kind of magnetic push (F = qvB). Because the force always acts sideways to the motion, it bends the charge into a circle rather than speeding it up — which is how particle accelerators and old TV tubes steer electrons.

A stationary charge in a magnetic field feels no force, but a charge moving with velocity v feels:

F = qvB·sinθ  (vector: F = q v × B)

θ is the angle between v and B. Direction: Fleming's left hand for a positive charge, Fleming's right hand for a negative charge (Thumb = Force, First finger = Field, Second finger = Velocity).

When v ⊥ B the charge moves in a circle: the magnetic force provides the centripetal force, F_B = F_c, so qvB = mv²/r and

r = mv / Bq

5.5 — Force between two parallel wires▶
🟢 In plain words: Each wire makes a field, and each sits in the other's field — so two current-carrying wires push or pull on each other. Simple rule: same-direction currents attract, opposite-direction currents repel. This exact effect is what officially defines the ampere.

Two parallel conductors carrying currents I₁ and I₂ separated by d exert equal and opposite forces on each other. The field of wire 1 at wire 2 is B₁ = μ₀I₁/2πd, so the force on length ℓ is F₁₂ = I₂ℓB₁:

|F₁₂| = |F₂₁| = F = μ₀I₁I₂ℓ / 2πd

Force per unit length:

F/ℓ = μ₀I₁I₂ / 2πd
Currents in the same direction → wires attract. Currents in opposite directions → wires repel.

Electromagnetic Induction

6.1 — Induced EMF▶
🟢 In plain words: We saw electricity makes magnetism — now the reverse: a changing magnetic field makes electricity. Wave a magnet near a coil and you generate a voltage. The catch is it must be changing — a still magnet does nothing; faster movement makes more voltage. This is the idea behind every generator.

Faraday looked for evidence that a magnetic field induces a current using a coil, battery, switch and galvanometer. He found no current when the current was steady, but a current was induced when the switch was turned on or off.

Moving a bar magnet relative to a coil:

  • Magnet stationary → galvanometer shows no deflection (no current).
  • Magnet moved towards the coil → momentary deflection one way; moved away → deflects the opposite way.

Conclusion: when the magnetic field lines through a coil change, an induced emf exists across the coil. The magnitude depends on the speed of relative motion — v increases → induced emf increases (induced emf ∝ v).

6.2.1 — Magnetic flux▶
🟢 In plain words: Flux is just "how many field lines pass through a loop." It's biggest when the loop faces the field straight-on and zero when the loop is edge-on. Changing this flux is exactly what creates induced voltage — so flux is the key quantity for the rest of this chapter.

Magnetic flux is the scalar product of the magnetic flux density B and the area vector A:

Φ = B·A = BA·cosϕ  (unit: weber, Wb = T·m²)

ϕ is the angle between B and the area vector A (which is always normal to the surface). Φ is maximum (= BA) when ϕ = 0° (B ⊥ surface, parallel to A), and zero when ϕ = 90°. Flux ∝ number of field lines through the area.

WORKED EXAMPLE 1 (from slides)

A single circular coil of diameter 3.0 cm sits in a uniform field. The plane of the coil makes 30° with the field. The flux is 1.20 mWb. Find B.

1A = πd²/4 = π(3.0×10⁻²)²/4 = 7.07×10⁻⁴ m²
2ϕ = 90° − 30° = 60° (angle to area vector).
3Φ = BA·cosϕ → 1.20×10⁻³ = B(7.07×10⁻⁴)cos60° → B = 3.40 T
6.2 — Faraday's Law of Induction▶
🟢 In plain words: Faraday's law puts a number on it: the faster the flux changes, the bigger the voltage (and more coil turns multiply it). You can change the flux three ways — change the field strength, the loop's area, or its angle. The minus sign is just Lenz's law (next lesson) reminding us the effect opposes the change.

Faraday's law: the magnitude of the induced emf is proportional to the rate of change of magnetic flux. The minus sign (Lenz's law) shows the induced emf opposes the change producing it.

ε = −dΦ/dt  and for N turns  ε = −N·dΦ/dt = −N(Φ_f − Φ_i)/dt

For a coil in series with a resistor R: ε = IR. Three factors change the flux: change in B, change in area A, or change in orientation angle θ:

ε = −NA·cosθ·(ΔB)/Δt | ε = −NB·cosθ·(ΔA)/Δt | ε = −NBA·(Δcosθ)/Δt
WORKED EXAMPLE 2 (from slides)

A coil of area 8.0 cm² and 50 turns lies perpendicular to a 0.20 T field. The field is reduced to zero in 0.50 s. Find (a) the initial flux linkage, (b) the induced emf.

1(a) NΦᵢ = NBᵢA·cos0° = 50·0.20·8.0×10⁻⁴ = 8.0×10⁻³ Wb
2(b) ε = −NA·cosϕ·(B_f − Bᵢ)/dt = −50·8.0×10⁻⁴·(0−0.20)/0.50 = 1.6×10⁻² V
WORKED EXAMPLE 3 (from slides)

1(a) dA = (π/4)(d_f² − dᵢ²) = (π/4)((5.3×10⁻²)² − (4.0×10⁻²)²) = 9.5×10⁻⁴ m²
2(b) ε = NB·cosϕ·(dA/dt) = 10·1.2·(9.5×10⁻⁴/0.25) = 4.56×10⁻² V
3I = ε/R = 4.56×10⁻²/2.4 = 1.9×10⁻² A

6.2.2 — Lenz's Law▶
🟢 In plain words: Lenz's law tells you which way the induced current flows: it always opposes whatever change created it ("nature pushes back"). Push a magnet in, the coil pushes it away; pull it out, the coil tries to hold it. It's really just energy conservation — you have to do work to generate electricity.

Lenz's law: an induced emf is always in a direction that opposes the original change in flux that caused it (the current produces a field that tends to restore the changed flux). It is used to find the direction of the induced current.

To produce an induced current you need a closed conducting loop and an external flux through it that changes in time. Steps:

  1. Decide whether Φ_B = BA·cosθ is increasing, decreasing or unchanged.
  2. B_induced: if Φ_B is decreasing → same direction as external field; if increasing → opposite direction; if unchanged → zero.
  3. Use the right-hand rule on B_induced to get the induced current direction.

4(d) F = IℓB = 15×10⁻³·0.05·0.6 (×N loops) ≈ 0.045 N
6.3 — EMF induced in a moving conductor▶
🟢 In plain words: Just sliding a straight wire through a magnetic field generates a voltage across its ends — no magnet-waving needed. The faster you move it, the longer the wire, and the stronger the field, the bigger the voltage: ε = Bℓv. (Even an aircraft's wings do this flying through Earth's field.)

A straight conductor of length ℓ moving with velocity v perpendicular to a field B sweeps area A = ℓx, giving Φ = Bℓx. By Faraday's law, ε = dΦ/dt = Bℓ(dx/dt) = Bℓv. In general:

ε = Bℓv·sinθ  (θ = angle between v and B)

6.4 — Electric Generators▶
🟢 In plain words: A generator is induction put to work: spin a coil in a magnetic field and the constantly-changing flux produces a voltage that smoothly rises and falls — that's where AC mains electricity comes from. Spin it faster (bigger ω) or use more turns/stronger field → bigger voltage.

A rectangular coil of N turns, area A, rotating at constant angular velocity ω in a field B has flux Φ = BA·cosωt. By Faraday's law the induced emf is:

ε = NBAω·sinωt  with  ε_max = NBAω,  ω = 2πf = 2π/T

It can also be written ε = NBAω·sinϕ (ϕ = angle between A and B). A coil rotating at constant ω produces a sinusoidally alternating emf — the basis of the generator/dynamo.

6.5 — Transformers & power transmission▶
🟢 In plain words: A transformer changes one AC voltage into another using two coils. More turns on the output coil = higher voltage (step-up); fewer = lower (step-down). Voltage up means current down (power stays the same). This is why power is sent across the country at super-high voltage — high voltage means low current, which means far less wasted heat in the wires.

A transformer is two coils linked by an iron core; a changing emf in the primary induces an emf in the secondary. The emf ratio equals the turns ratio, and (energy conserved) the current ratio is the inverse:

V_S/V_P = N_S/N_P  and  I_S/I_P = N_P/N_S  |  P = VI

Step-up: V_S > V_P (more secondary turns). Transformers work only with changing (ac) current — one reason electricity is transmitted as ac, stepped up to high voltage to cut transmission loss (P_loss = I²R).

6.6 — Self-Inductance▶
🟢 In plain words: A coil's own changing current creates a changing field through itself, which induces a "back-voltage" that resists the change. So a coil (inductor) is sluggish — it doesn't like sudden changes in current. "Inductance" (L, in henrys) measures how strongly it resists. It's the electrical version of inertia.

Self-induction: producing an induced emf in a coil due to a change of current in the same coil. By Lenz's law the induced (back) emf opposes the change — when I increases the induced emf opposes the current; when I decreases it acts in the same direction. (Demonstrated by a lamp with an iron-core coil brightening slowly vs. a lamp with a resistor lighting instantly.)

Φ_L = LI,  ε = −L·dI/dt,  L = NΦ/I = Φ_L/I  (unit: henry, H)

For an air-core solenoid: L = μ₀N²A/ℓ. The inductor stores energy in its magnetic field.

6.7 — Energy stored in a magnetic field▶
🟢 In plain words: Just as a capacitor stores energy in its electric field, an inductor stores energy in its magnetic field while current flows: U = ½LI². The two formulas are mirror images (½CV² vs ½LI²).

Building current against the back emf stores energy. Integrating dU = LI·dI from 0 to I:

U = ½LI²  (analogous to U = ½CV² for a capacitor)

For a long air-core solenoid (L = μ₀N²A/ℓ): U = ½(μ₀N²AI²/ℓ). The energy density of a magnetic field is u = ½B²/μ₀.

6.8 — Mutual Inductance▶
🟢 In plain words: Put two coils near each other and a changing current in one induces a voltage in the other — they "talk" through their shared magnetic field. "Mutual inductance" (M) measures how strongly they're coupled. This is exactly how a transformer (and wireless phone charging) works.

Mutual induction: producing an induced emf in one coil due to the change of current in another coil that is magnetically linked. For coils with N₁, N₂ turns:

ε₂ = −M·dI₁/dt,  ε₁ = −M·dI₂/dt,  M = N₂Φ₂/I₁ = N₁Φ₁/I₂  (unit: H)

For two coaxial solenoids with no flux leakage: M = μ₀N₁N₂A/ℓ.

Electromagnetic Waves

7.1 — Electromagnetic Waves▶
🟢 In plain words: A changing electric field makes a magnetic field, which makes an electric field, and so on — so the two fields keep regenerating each other and ride off through space as a wave. The E and B fields wiggle at right angles to each other and to the direction of travel. Light, radio, and X-rays are all this same kind of wave.

Since a changing electric field produces a magnetic field, and a changing magnetic field produces an electric field, once sinusoidal fields are created they can propagate on their own. These propagating fields are called electromagnetic waves.

The electric field E and magnetic field B are perpendicular to each other and to the direction of propagation (a transverse wave). The whole pattern moves in the direction perpendicular to both E and B.

7.2 — EM waves and their speed▶
🟢 In plain words: All EM waves travel at the same speed in a vacuum — the speed of light, c = 3×10⁸ m/s. The basic wave relation v = fλ applies (speed = frequency × wavelength), so frequency and wavelength trade off. Remarkably, this speed pops straight out of the electric and magnetic constants — that's how we know light is an EM wave.

For a wave of wavelength λ and frequency f, the fields are E = E₀·sin(kx − ωt) and B = B₀·sin(kx − ωt), with:

k = 2π/λ,  ω = 2πf,  v = fλ = ω/k,  E/B = v

From Maxwell's equations the speed in vacuum is:

v = c = 1/√(ε₀μ₀) = 3.0×10⁸ m/s  (exactly the measured speed of light)
WORKED EXAMPLE 1 (from slides)

A 60-Hz EM wave propagates in the z direction with E along x, E₀ = 2.0 V/m. Write E and B as functions of position and time.

1ω = 2πf = 2π(60) = 120π rad/s. λ = c/f = 3×10⁸/60 = 5×10⁶ m.
2k = 2π/λ = 2π/(5×10⁶) = 1.25×10⁻⁶ m⁻¹.
3E_x = 2.0·sin(1.25×10⁻⁶ z − 120πt) V/m.
4B₀ = E₀/c = 2/(3×10⁸) = 6.67×10⁻⁹ T; B_y = 6.67×10⁻⁹·sin(1.25×10⁻⁶ z − 120πt) T.
7.3 — Light & the electromagnetic spectrum▶
🟢 In plain words: The "EM spectrum" is the whole family of these waves, sorted by wavelength: radio → microwave → infrared → visible light → ultraviolet → X-ray → gamma. They're all the same thing, just different wavelengths — and the rainbow of visible light is only a tiny slice in the middle. Use c = fλ to convert between frequency and wavelength.

The frequency of an EM wave is related to its wavelength and the speed of light:

c = λf

EM waves can have any wavelength; we name the parts of the spectrum (long→short wavelength / low→high frequency): radio waves, microwaves, infrared, visible light, ultraviolet, X-rays, gamma rays. Visible light spans λ ≈ 7.5×10⁻⁷ m (red, f = 4×10¹⁴ Hz) to 4.0×10⁻⁷ m (violet, f = 7.5×10¹⁴ Hz).

Light: Reflection & Refraction

8.1 — The Ray Model of Light▶
🟢 In plain words: For everyday optics (mirrors, lenses) we don't need the wave details — we just treat light as straight arrows called "rays." This simple picture is enough to predict reflections and bending, and it's the foundation for the rest of this topic.

Light is a form of electromagnetic radiation. It very often travels in straight-line paths called light rays. We represent light using rays — straight lines emanating from an object. This is an idealization but very useful for geometric optics.

8.2 — Reflection & plane mirrors▶
🟢 In plain words: Light bounces off a surface so that the angle in equals the angle out (measured from the normal — the line at 90° to the surface). A flat mirror gives a "virtual" image that looks like it's the same distance behind the glass as you are in front, same size, left-right swapped.

Reflection is the return of all or part of a beam of light when it meets the boundary between two media. Laws of reflection:

  • The incident ray, the reflected ray and the normal all lie in the same plane.
  • Angle of incidence = angle of reflection: θᵢ = θ_r.

A rough surface gives diffuse reflection (eye sees light at all angles); a smooth surface (mirror) gives specular reflection (eye must be in the right position).

A plane mirror forms a virtual image that appears behind the mirror, the same size as the object, with image distance = object distance.

WORKED EXAMPLE 1 (from slides)

Two flat mirrors are perpendicular. An incoming beam makes 15° with the first mirror. What angle does the outgoing beam make with the second mirror?

1Tracing the angles through both reflections, the outgoing beam makes 15° with the second mirror (it emerges anti-parallel to the incoming beam).

8.2.1 — Spherical mirrors▶
🟢 In plain words: Curved mirrors come in two types: concave (caves inward, focuses light — like a makeup/shaving mirror) and convex (bulges out, spreads light — like a shop security mirror). One handy formula, 1/f = 1/d₀ + 1/dᵢ, locates the image; just be careful with the +/− sign rules.

A spherical mirror is a reflecting surface that is part of a sphere: concave (converging) or convex (diverging). Terms: centre of curvature C, radius of curvature r, pole/vertex P, principal axis. The focal length is half the radius of curvature:

f = r/2

Concave focus is real; convex focus is virtual. Mirror equation and magnification:

1/f = 1/d₀ + 1/dᵢ   m = hᵢ/h₀ = −dᵢ/d₀
Sign convention: object/image distance positive = real (in front of mirror), negative = virtual (behind). Focal length: + for concave, − for convex. A convex mirror always gives a virtual, upright, diminished image (used as a driving mirror). A concave mirror gives a real inverted image when d₀ > F.

8.3 — Refraction, index & Snell's law▶
🟢 In plain words: Light slows down and bends when it crosses into a denser material (air → water → glass) — that's why a straw looks broken in a glass of water. The "refractive index" n says how much it slows, and Snell's law (n₁ sin i = n₂ sin r) tells you exactly how much it bends.

Refractive index n is the ratio of light speeds; absolute index n = c/v (dimensionless, >1; e.g. water 1.33, crown glass 1.52, diamond 2.42). Refraction is the change of direction and speed of a ray passing between media.

Snell's law:

n₁·sin i = n₂·sin r   (sin i / sin r = n₂/n₁)

Going into a denser medium (n₂ > n₁) the ray bends toward the normal (r < i); into a less dense medium it bends away. For apparent depth viewed straight down: n = real depth / apparent depth.

Lenses

9-1 Thin Lenses▶
🟢 In plain words: A lens bends light to form images. Two kinds: converging (fat in the middle — pulls light to a point, like a magnifying glass) and diverging (thin in the middle — spreads light out). The focal point is where parallel light gets focused, and the focal length f is its distance from the lens.

A thin lens is a transparent material with two spherical refracting surfaces whose thickness is thin compared to the radii of curvature of the two refracting surfaces.

There are two types of thin lens: converging and diverging.

  • Any lens that is thicker at its centre than at its edges is a converging lens.
  • Any lens that is thicker at its edges than at its centre is a diverging lens.

(a) Converging (convex) lenses — Biconvex (r₁ +ve, r₂ +ve), Plano-convex (r₁ +ve, r₂ ∞), Convex meniscus (r₁ +ve, r₂ −ve).

(b) Diverging (concave) lenses — Biconcave (r₁ −ve, r₂ −ve), Plano-concave (r₁ −ve, r₂ ∞), Concave meniscus (r₁ +ve, r₂ −ve).

Terms of thin lenses: Centre of curvature (C₁, C₂) — centre of the sphere of which the surface is a part. Radius of curvature (r₁, r₂) — radius of that sphere. Principal (optical) axis — the line joining the two centres of curvature. Optical centre (O) — the point at which any rays entering the lens pass without deviation.

Focus (F₁, F₂): for a converging lens it is the point on the principal axis where parallel rays converge after passing through the lens (its focus is real); for a diverging lens it is the point from which parallel rays seem to diverge after passing through the lens (its focus is virtual).

Focal length (f): the distance between the focus F and the optical centre O of the lens.

9-1-1 Ray Tracing▶
🟢 In plain words: To find where a lens forms an image, you draw 3 special, easy-to-predict rays from the top of the object: one through the centre (goes straight), one parallel to the axis (bends through the focus), and one through the focus (comes out parallel). Wherever they cross is where the image sits.

The three principal rays used to locate an image:

1Ray 1 — a ray parallel to the axis emerges passing through the second focal point F₂ of a converging lens, or appears to come from F₂ of a diverging lens.
2Ray 2 — a ray through the centre of the lens is not appreciably deviated (emerges along essentially the same line).
3Ray 3 — a ray through (or proceeding toward) the first focal point F₁ emerges parallel to the axis.

The intersection of these rays (at least any two) determines the position and size of the image.

Diverging (concave) lens — image is always: virtual, upright, diminished, and formed in front of the lens, for an object at any position.

Image produced by a convex lens:

  • d₀ > 2f → real, inverted, smaller than object
  • d₀ = 2f → real, inverted, same size as object
  • 2f > d₀ > f → real, inverted, bigger than object
  • d₀ = f → real, inverted, bigger, image at infinity
  • d₀ < f → virtual, upright, bigger than object

Image produced by a concave lens: any object position → virtual, upright, smaller than object.

9-1-2 Thin Lens Equation & Magnification▶
🟢 In plain words: Instead of drawing rays, you can just calculate. The lens equation 1/d₀ + 1/dᵢ = 1/f finds where the image is, and magnification m = −dᵢ/d₀ tells you how big and which way up. Getting the +/− signs right is the whole game (negative image distance = virtual image, negative m = upside-down).

Thin lens equation: 1/d₀ + 1/dᵢ = 1/f.   Magnification: m = hᵢ/h₀ = −dᵢ/d₀.   Power: P = 1/f, measured in dioptres (1 D = 1 m⁻¹), positive if converging, negative if diverging.

Sign conventions: object location u — +ve in front of lens; image location v — +ve behind lens (real), −ve in front (virtual); image height hᵢ — +ve upright, −ve inverted; focal length f — +ve converging, −ve diverging.

EXAMPLE 1 — image formed by converging lens

2m = −dᵢ/d₀ = hᵢ/h₀ → −0.05/1.0 = hᵢ/0.076 → hᵢ = −3.8×10⁻³ m.

9-2 Combinations of Lenses▶
🟢 In plain words: Microscopes and telescopes use two lenses in a row. The trick: the image made by the first lens becomes the "object" for the second. Solve each lens one at a time with the lens equation, and the total magnification is just the two magnifications multiplied together.

Many instruments (microscopes, telescopes) use two converging lenses. The 1st lens (closest to the object) is the objective; the 2nd (closest to the eye) is the eyepiece/ocular. The image formed by the 1st lens is the object for the 2nd lens, and the final image is the image formed by the 2nd lens.

Apply the thin lens formula to each lens separately. The overall magnification is the product of the separate magnifications: m = m₁ × m₂.

EXAMPLE 4 — compound microscope

2Object for eyepiece: d₀₂ = d − dᵢ₁ = 61.0 − 39.7 = 21.3 mm.
3Eyepiece: 1/25.5 = 1/21.3 + 1/dᵢ₂ → dᵢ₂ = −129 mm (in front of the 2nd lens).
4m = |39.7/24.1| × |−129/21.3| = 9.98.
5Final image: virtual, inverted, magnified, formed in front of the 1st and 2nd lenses.
EXAMPLE 5 — a two-lens system

Two converging lenses A and B, f_A = 20.0 cm and f_B = 25.0 cm, are 80.0 cm apart. An object is placed 60.0 cm in front of the first lens. Find (a) the final image position and (b) the magnification.

1Lens A: 1/20 = 1/60 + 1/dᵢ → dᵢ = 30 cm (real); m₁ = −30/60 = −0.5 (inverted, diminished).
2Object for B: d₀ = 80 − 30 = 50 cm. 1/25 = 1/50 + 1/dᵢ → dᵢ = 50 cm; m₂ = −50/50 = −1.
3m = m₁ × m₂ = (−0.5)(−1) = 0.5 → diminished, upright, real image.
9-3 Lensmaker's Equation▶
🟢 In plain words: This formula lets a lens manufacturer work out the focal length from the physical lens itself — how curved its two faces are and what glass it's made of. More curved surfaces or higher refractive index → shorter focal length (stronger lens).

Relates the radii of curvature and the refractive index to the focal length. In air:

1/f = (n − 1)(1/r₁ + 1/r₂)

In a surrounding medium (general form):

1/f = (n_lens/n_medium − 1)(1/r₁ + 1/r₂)

EXAMPLE 6 — biconvex camera lens

A person of height 1.75 m stands 2.50 m in front of a camera with a thin biconvex lens, radii of curvature 7.69 mm, crown glass n = 1.52. Find (a) f, (c) the image position and height, (d) characteristics.

1r₁ = r₂ = +7.69×10⁻³ m. 1/f = (1.52−1)(1/7.69×10⁻³ + 1/7.69×10⁻³) → f = +7.39×10⁻³ m.
21/7.39×10⁻³ = 1/2.50 + 1/dᵢ → dᵢ = 7.41×10⁻³ m (at the back of the lens).
3m = hᵢ/h₀ = |dᵢ/d₀|: hᵢ/1.75 = |7.41×10⁻³/2.50| → hᵢ = 5.19×10⁻³ m (5.19 mm).
4Image: real, inverted, diminished, formed at the back of the lens.
EXAMPLE 7 — plano-convex lens

A thin plano-convex lens of glass n = 1.66. When an object is set 10 cm from the lens, a virtual image ten times its size is formed. Find (a) f and (b) the radius of curvature of the convex surface.

1m = |dᵢ/d₀| = 10 → dᵢ = 10d₀; virtual → dᵢ = −10d₀ = −100 cm.
21/f = 1/10 + 1/(−100) → f = +11.1 cm.
3Plano-convex → r₂ = ∞. 1/11.1 = (1.66−1)(1/r₁ + 1/∞) → r₁ = +7.33 cm.
EXAMPLE 8 — concave meniscus, air vs water

A thin concave meniscus lens, material n = 3/2, faces of radii 20 cm and 10 cm. Find f (a) in air, (b) immersed in water n = 4/3. Here r₁ = +20 cm, r₂ = −10 cm.

1In air: 1/f = (3/2 − 1)(1/+20 + 1/−10) → f = −40 cm.
2In water: 1/f = (n₂/n₁ − 1)(1/r₁ + 1/r₂) = ((3/2)/(4/3) − 1)(1/+20 + 1/−10) → f = −160 cm.

Interference & Diffraction

10-1 Huygens' Principle & Law of Refraction▶
🟢 In plain words: Huygens' clever idea: treat every point on a wave as a tiny new source of ripples; add them all up and you get the next position of the wave. This neatly explains why waves bend around corners (diffraction) and bend when they slow down (refraction).

Wavefront: a line or surface, in the path of a wave motion, on which the disturbances at every point have the same phase. A wavefront is always perpendicular to the direction of wave propagation.

Huygens' principle: every point on a wavefront can be considered as a source of secondary wavelets that spread out in the forward direction at the speed of the wave. The new wavefront is the envelope (tangent) of all the secondary wavelets.

The bending of waves behind obstacles is called diffraction. Diffraction occurs (a) around the edge of an obstacle, (b) through a large hole, (c) through a small hole whose size is on the order of the wavelength.

Huygens' principle also explains refraction: wavelets travel more slowly in the medium of higher refractive index, bending the wavefront. The frequency stays the same, but the wavelength changes:

λ₂/λ₁ = v₂/v₁ = n₁/n₂   and   λₙ = λ/n

Highway mirages arise from a gradually changing index of refraction in heated air (hot air near the road is less dense → lower index → light bends and seems reflected off the road).

10-2 Interference — Young's Double Slit▶
🟢 In plain words: Shine light through two narrow slits and you get a striped pattern of bright and dark bands on a screen. Why? Where the two light waves arrive in step they add up (bright); where they're out of step they cancel (dark). This was the famous proof that light behaves as a wave. The bright bands are evenly spaced by Δy = λL/d.

Monochromatic light through a single slit S diffracts and reaches two slits S₁, S₂, which act as two coherent sources in phase (same wavefront, equal distance from S). They superpose to form bright (constructive) and dark (destructive) fringes on the screen.

The path difference between the two slits is d·sinθ. With d = slit separation, L = distance to screen, m = order (0, ±1, ±2, …):

Bright (max): d·sinθ = mλ  →  yₘ = mλL/d

Dark (min): d·sinθ = (m+½)λ  →  xₘ = (m+½)λL/d

Fringe separation (consecutive bright or dark): Δy = λL/d

Bright: m=0 central (zeroth order), m=±1 first order, …   Dark: m=0 → 1st dark (zeroth order min), m=±1 → 2nd dark (1st order min), …

EXAMPLE 1

A double-slit pattern is viewed on a screen 1.00 m from the slits. The third-order minima are 25.0 cm apart. Find (a) the ratio λ/d, and (b) the distance between the 1st-order minimum and 4th-order maximum.

1x₃ = Δx₃/2 = 0.25/2 = 0.125 m. Using x₃ = (3+½)λL/d: 0.125 = 3.5·λ(1.00)/d → λ/d = 3.57×10⁻².
2x₁ = (1+½)λL/d = 1.5 λL/d; y₄ = 4λL/d. Δd = y₄ − x₁ = 2.5 λL/d = 2.5(3.57×10⁻²)(1.00) = 8.93×10⁻² m.
EXAMPLE 2

With λ₁ = 600 nm the fringe separation is 7.0 mm. With a second source the fringe separation is 5.0 mm. Find the second wavelength.

1Δy = λL/d → Δy₂/Δy₁ = λ₂/λ₁: (5.0×10⁻³)/(7.0×10⁻³) = λ₂/(600×10⁻⁹).
2λ₂ = 429×10⁻⁹ m (429 nm).
EXAMPLE 3

Two coherent sources, d = 1.2 mm, screen L = 2.5 m away. (a) For f = 5.77×10¹⁴ Hz find (i) λ, (ii) the bright-fringe separation in air. (b) In water (n = 1.33), find the dark-fringe separation. (c = 3.00×10⁸ m/s.)

1c = fλ → λ = 3.00×10⁸/5.77×10¹⁴ = 520×10⁻⁹ m (520 nm).
2Δy = λL/d = (520×10⁻⁹)(2.5)/(1.2×10⁻³) = 1.08×10⁻³ m.
3In water: λ_w = λ/n = 520×10⁻⁹/1.33 = 3.91×10⁻⁷ m → Δx = λ_w L/d = 8.15×10⁻⁴ m.
EXAMPLE 4

Two slits 0.100 mm apart, screen 1.20 m away, λ = 500 nm. How far apart are adjacent bright fringes?

1Δy = λL/d = (500×10⁻⁹)(1.2)/(0.1×10⁻³) = 6×10⁻³ m (6 mm).
10-3 Diffraction by a Single Slit▶
🟢 In plain words: Even a single slit spreads light into a pattern: one wide, bright central band with dimmer bands either side. Counter-intuitively, a narrower slit spreads the light out more. The dark gaps fall where a·sinθ = nλ.

Diffraction is the superposition of secondary wavelets from a continuous section of wavefront limited by an aperture or obstacle. The central fringe is bright, widest, and most intense; intensity falls off for higher orders.

With a = slit width, L = distance to screen (L ≫ a), n = order (1, 2, 3, …):

Minima (dark): a·sinθₙ = nλ  →  yₙ = nλL/a

Secondary maxima (bright): a·sinθₙ = (n+½)λ  →  yₙ = (n+½)λL/a

Central maximum: width w = 2λL/a; angular width θ_w = 2·sin⁻¹(λ/a)

Maximum number of orders: set θ = 90°, so for minima m_max = a/λ. Both sinθ ∝ λ and yₙ ∝ λ.

EXAMPLE 6

Sodium light λ = 580 nm through a slit, screen 0.60 m away, central maximum width 5.0 cm. Find (a) the slit width, (b) the angular width of the central maximum, (c) the number of minima observed.

1w = 2λL/a → 5.0×10⁻² = 2(580×10⁻⁹)(0.60)/a → a = 1.39×10⁻⁵ m.
2θ_w = 2·sin⁻¹(λ/a) = 2·sin⁻¹(580×10⁻⁹/1.39×10⁻⁵) = 4.78°.
3a·sin90° = n_max·λ → n_max = 1.39×10⁻⁵/580×10⁻⁹ = 23.97 ≈ 23. Total = 23 × 2 = 46 fringes.
10-4 Diffraction Grating▶
🟢 In plain words: A diffraction grating is like the double-slit but with thousands of slits — so the bright spots become razor-sharp and well separated. That makes it perfect for splitting light into precise colours (the rainbow you see on a CD is a grating effect). Same formula: d·sinθ = nλ.

A diffraction grating is a large number of equally spaced parallel slits. With N lines per unit length the slit separation is d = 1/N. Transmission gratings are the usual type; reflection gratings include CDs/DVDs. The pattern is much narrower and sharper than a double slit.

Maxima: d·sinθₙ = nλ,   n = 0, ±1, ±2, …    Max orders: n_max = d/λ (at θ = 90°)

With white light, each higher order spreads into a rainbow (central fringe stays white).

EXAMPLE 7

Monochromatic light falls normally on a grating of 3000 lines/cm. The angular separation between the two first-order maxima is 35°. Find (a) the wavelength, (b) the angular separation between the 2nd- and 3rd-order maxima.

1θ₁ = 35°/2 = 17.5°; d = 1/N = 1/3000 cm = 3.33×10⁻⁶ m.
2d·sinθ₁ = λ → (3.33×10⁻⁶)sin17.5° = 1.00×10⁻⁶ m.
3d·sinθ₂ = 2λ → θ₂ = 36.9°; d·sinθ₃ = 3λ → θ₃ = 64.3°. Δθ₂₃ = 64.3° − 36.9° = 27.4°.
EXAMPLE 8

Find the angular positions of the 1st- and 2nd-order maxima for λ = 400 nm and 700 nm on a grating of 10,000 lines/cm. (d = 1×10⁻⁶ m.)

1400 nm: sinθ = nλ/d → 1st order 23.58°, 2nd order 53.13°.
2700 nm: 1st order 44.43°; 2nd order has sinθ = 1.4 > 1 → does not exist.

Diffraction vs interference: diffraction — secondary maxima unequal, central maximum wide, secondary minima not fully zero. Interference — all bright bands equal, all fringes equal width, dark fringes completely zero.

Early Quantum Theory & Models of the Atom

11-1 Planck's Quantum Hypothesis; Blackbody Radiation▶
🟢 In plain words: Classical physics couldn't explain the colour of glowing hot objects. Planck's radical fix: energy doesn't flow smoothly but comes in tiny fixed packets ("quanta"), each of size E = hf. This was the birth of quantum physics — energy is "pixelated," not continuous.

A black body is an ideal system that absorbs all radiation incident on it; the radiation it emits is black-body radiation. Total intensity is proportional to the fourth power of temperature: I = P/A = σeT⁴.

The peak wavelength shifts to shorter wavelengths as temperature rises (Wien's displacement law):

λ_P · T = 2.90×10⁻³ m·K

Classical theories (Rayleigh–Jeans, Wien) failed to fit the experimental curve at all wavelengths. In 1900 Planck proposed that EM radiation from a black body is emitted in discrete packets (quanta); energy is quantised and depends on frequency:

E = hf = hc/λ   (h = 6.63×10⁻³⁴ J·s)    Eₙ = nhf

1 eV = 1.60×10⁻¹⁹ J. In 1905 Einstein extended this: EM radiation consists of particle-like packets called photons.

EXAMPLE 1 — Sun's surface temperature

The Sun's peak intensity is at about 500 nm. Estimate its surface temperature.

1T = 2.9×10⁻³/λ_P = 2.9×10⁻³/(500×10⁻⁹) = ≈ 5800 K.
EXAMPLE 3 — green-light photon

Green light has λ = 740 nm. Find (a) its frequency, (b) its photon energy in joules and eV.

1c = fλ → f = 3.00×10⁸/(740×10⁻⁹) = 4.05×10¹⁴ Hz.
2E = hf = (6.63×10⁻³⁴)(4.05×10¹⁴) = 2.69×10⁻¹⁹ J = 2.69×10⁻¹⁹/1.60×10⁻¹⁹ = 1.66 eV.
EXAMPLE 4 — gamma photon

Gamma radiation of λ = 4.62×10⁻¹² m. Find the photon energy in eV.

1E = hc/λ = (6.63×10⁻³⁴)(3.00×10⁸)/(4.62×10⁻¹²) = 4.31×10⁻¹⁴ J = 2.69×10⁵ eV.
11-2 Photon Theory & the Photoelectric Effect▶
🟢 In plain words: Shine light on a metal and it can knock electrons out — but only if the light's frequency is high enough; dim blue light works while bright red light doesn't. This only makes sense if light comes in packets (photons), each carrying energy hf. One photon must carry enough energy (beat the "work function") to free one electron. This won Einstein the Nobel Prize.

The photoelectric effect is the emission of electrons from a metal surface when EM radiation of sufficiently high frequency strikes it. The emitted electron is a photoelectron. The effect does not occur if the frequency is too low.

The work function W₀ is the minimum energy needed to release an electron: W₀ = hf₀ = hc/λ₀, where f₀ is the threshold frequency and λ₀ the threshold wavelength.

Einstein's photoelectric equation:

hf = ½mv²(max) + W₀   (E = K_max + W₀)

Cases: hf > W₀ (f > f₀) → electron emitted with max KE; hf = W₀ → emitted with zero KE; hf < W₀ → no electron emitted. Sample work functions (eV): Al 4.3, Na 2.3, Cu 4.7, Au 5.1, Ag 4.3.

EXAMPLE 5 — photon energy (blue light)

Find the energy of a blue-light photon, λ = 450 nm.

1E = hc/λ = (6.63×10⁻³⁴)(3×10⁸)/(450×10⁻⁹) = 4.42×10⁻¹⁹ J.
EXAMPLE 5b — photons from a lightbulb

How many visible photons does a 100-W bulb emit per second at 3% efficiency (λ ≈ 500 nm)?

1Useful power = 100 × 0.03 = 3 J/s. E_photon = hc/λ ≈ 3.98×10⁻¹⁹ J.
2n = 3 / E_photon ≈ 7.5×10¹⁸ photons/s.
EXAMPLE 6 — photoelectron from sodium (W₀ = 2.28 eV)

Find the KE and speed of electrons ejected by light of (a) 410 nm, (b) 550 nm.

1(a) f = 7.32×10¹⁴ Hz; E = hf = 4.853×10⁻¹⁹ J = 3.033 eV. K_max = E − W₀ = 3.033 − 2.28 = 0.753 eV = 1.24×10⁻¹⁹ J; v = 5.23×10⁵ m/s.
2(b) f = 5.45×10¹⁴ Hz; E = hf = 3.61×10⁻¹⁹ J = 2.26 eV < W₀ → no electron emitted.
11-3 Energy, Mass & Momentum of a Photon▶
🟢 In plain words: A photon has no mass, yet it still carries momentum (p = h/λ) — so light can actually push on things. That tiny push is real: it's the idea behind solar sails. Shorter wavelength = more momentum and energy.

A photon travels at the speed of light, so its rest mass must be zero. Its momentum follows from the relativistic energy–momentum relation:

p = E/c = hf/c = h/λ

EXAMPLE 7 — photon momentum & force

The 10¹⁹ photons/s (λ = 500 nm) from a 100-W bulb are all absorbed by black paper. Find (a) one photon's momentum, (b) the force on the paper.

1p = h/λ = (6.63×10⁻³⁴)/(500×10⁻⁹) = 1.33×10⁻²⁷ kg·m/s.
2F = Δp/Δt = nh/(λt) = ≈ 1.33×10⁻⁸ N.
11-4 Wave–Particle Duality & Complementarity▶
🟢 In plain words: Is light a wave or a particle? Both. Some experiments (interference) show wave behaviour; others (photoelectric effect) show particle behaviour. It's not one or the other — light shows whichever face the experiment asks for. This is "wave–particle duality."

Diffraction and interference show light is a wave; the photoelectric and Compton effects show it is a particle. Light has a dual wave–particle nature.

The principle of complementarity: both the wave and particle aspects are fundamental to light's nature. The de Broglie relation links them:

λ = h/p = h/mv

Wave evidence: Young's double slit, diffraction.   Particle evidence: photoelectric effect, Compton effect.

11-5 Wave Nature of Matter▶
🟢 In plain words: If light (a wave) can act like a particle, de Broglie asked: can particles act like waves? Yes — everything has a wavelength λ = h/mv. For big objects it's far too tiny to notice, but for tiny electrons it's significant, which is why electrons diffract and why electron microscopes work.

The wave nature of matter is most important for very light particles such as electrons. Electron wavelengths can be on the order of 10⁻¹⁰ m, so electrons can be diffracted by crystals like X-rays. de Broglie: λ = h/mv. For an electron accelerated through a voltage V, ½mv² = eV.

EXAMPLE 8 — photon momentum & energy

Light of λ = 550 nm on a sodium surface. Find the photon's momentum and energy.

1p = h/λ = (6.63×10⁻³⁴)/(550×10⁻⁹) = 1.21×10⁻²⁷ kg·m/s.
2E = hc/λ = 3.62×10⁻¹⁹ J.
EXAMPLE 9 — de Broglie wavelengths

Find λ for (a) a 77-kg jogger at 4.1 m/s, (b) an electron (9.11×10⁻³¹ kg) at 3.25×10⁵ m/s.

1(a) λ = h/mv = (6.63×10⁻³⁴)/(77×4.1) = 2.1×10⁻³⁶ m.
2(b) λ = (6.63×10⁻³⁴)/(9.11×10⁻³¹×3.25×10⁵) = 2.24×10⁻⁹ m.
EXAMPLE 10 — wavelength of a ball

Find λ for a 0.20-kg ball at 15 m/s.

1λ = h/mv = (6.63×10⁻³⁴)/(0.20×15) = 2.21×10⁻³⁴ m.

Nuclear Physics & Radioactivity

12-1 Structure & Properties of the Nucleus▶
🟢 In plain words: The nucleus is the tiny core of the atom, packed with protons and neutrons (together called nucleons). Z = number of protons (which element it is), A = total nucleons. Isotopes are the same element with different numbers of neutrons. Surprisingly, a nucleus weighs slightly less than its parts — that "missing mass" is the energy holding it together.

A nucleus is made of protons and neutrons (collectively nucleons). Proton charge +e (1.60×10⁻¹⁹ C), mass 1.672×10⁻²⁷ kg; neutron uncharged, mass 1.675×10⁻²⁷ kg. In a neutral atom, number of protons = number of electrons.

Atomic number Z = protons; neutron number N; mass number A = nucleons, with A = Z + N. A nuclide is written ᴬ_Z X.

Isotopes are nuclides with the same Z but different A (same protons/electrons, different neutrons) — e.g. ¹H, ²H (deuterium), ³H (tritium); ¹⁶O, ¹⁷O, ¹⁸O.

Nuclear radius: r ≈ (1.2×10⁻¹⁵ m)·A^(1/3). Masses are referenced to carbon-12 (exactly 12 u), where 1 u = 1.6605×10⁻²⁷ kg = 931.5 MeV/c². A stable nucleus has less mass than its separate nucleons; the missing mass becomes energy.

12-2 Binding Energy & Nuclear Forces▶
🟢 In plain words: That "missing mass" (the mass defect) equals the binding energy — how much energy you'd need to rip the nucleus apart — found with Einstein's E = mc². Divide by the number of nucleons and you get a stability score: iron-56 is the most tightly bound (most stable) nucleus. The glue itself is the super-strong but super-short-range "strong nuclear force."

Mass defect: Δm = (Z·m_p + N·m_n) − M_A. The binding energy (energy to separate all nucleons) is the energy equivalent of the mass defect:

E_B = (Δm)c²    Binding energy per nucleon = E_B / A

1 MeV = 1.60×10⁻¹³ J. The higher the binding energy per nucleon, the more stable the nucleus (⁵⁶Fe is near the peak). Nucleons are held by the short-range strong nuclear force (≈0 beyond ~10⁻¹⁵ m); the long-range Coulomb force is why high-Z nuclei need extra neutrons. The weak nuclear force governs beta decay.

EXAMPLE 2 — binding energy of ²⁷₁₃Al

Given m_n = 1.00867 u, m_p = 1.00782 u, M_Al = 26.98154 u. (Z = 13, N = 14.)

1Δm = [13(1.00782) + 14(1.00867)] − 26.98154 = 0.2415 u.
2Δm = 0.2415 × 1.66×10⁻²⁷ = 4.0089×10⁻²⁸ kg; E_B = Δmc² = 3.608×10⁻¹¹ J.
3E_B = 3.608×10⁻¹¹ / 1.60×10⁻¹³ = 226 MeV.
12-3 Radioactivity: alpha, beta, gamma▶
🟢 In plain words: Unstable nuclei spit out radiation to become more stable. Three types: alpha (a heavy helium nucleus — easily stopped by paper), beta (a fast electron — stopped by aluminium), and gamma (high-energy light — needs thick lead). Alpha and beta change the element; gamma just sheds extra energy.

Radioactivity is the random, spontaneous emission of radiation when an unstable nuclide decays — unplanned, unpredictable, independent of physical/chemical conditions.

Alpha (α): a helium nucleus ⁴₂He, +2e. ᴬ_Z X → ᴬ⁻⁴_(Z−2) Y + ⁴₂He   Q = M_P c² − (M_D + m_α)c².

Beta-minus (β⁻): an electron. ᴬ_Z X → ᴬ_(Z+1) Y + ⁰₋₁e + ν̄ (A unchanged, Z +1). Beta-plus (β⁺): a positron, Z −1.

Gamma (γ): high-energy photon, uncharged, zero mass; emitted when a nucleus drops from an excited state (X*) to a lower state — no change to Z or A.

Comparison: charge α +2e, β ∓1e, γ 0. Deflected by fields: α yes, β yes, γ no. Ionizing power: α strong, β moderate, γ weak. Penetration: α weak, β moderate, γ strong.

EXAMPLE 5 — uranium decay energy

²³²₉₂U (232.037156 u) → ²²⁸₉₀Th (228.028741 u) + α (4.002603 u). Find Q.

1Q = [232.037156 − (228.028741 + 4.002603)] u × c², converted to MeV = 5.41 MeV.
EXAMPLE 6 — energy in ¹⁴C β decay

¹⁴₆C → ¹⁴₇N + ⁰₋₁e + ν. m_P = 14.003242, m_D = 14.003074 (β mass negligible).

1Q = (m_P − m_D)c² = 0.000168 u × c² = 0.156 MeV.
12-4 Half-Life & Rate of Decay▶
🟢 In plain words: You can't predict when one nucleus decays, but for billions of them the pattern is precise. The half-life is the time for half of them to decay. After one half-life, 50% remain; after two, 25%; after three, 12.5% — a steady halving. "Activity" is just how many decays happen per second.

Decay constant λ (s⁻¹) — probability a nucleus decays per second: dN/dt = −λN. Integrating gives the exponential decay law:

N = N₀ e^(−λt)

Half-life T₁/₂ — time for half the nuclei to decay:

T₁/₂ = ln2 / λ = 0.693 / λ

Activity A (decays/s) = decay rate: A = λN = A₀ e^(−λt). Units: 1 Ci = 3.7×10¹⁰ decays/s; 1 Bq = 1 decay/s.

EXAMPLE 7 — nuclide B formed

A → B, T₁/₂ of A = 5.0 days, N₀ = 1.0×10²⁰. Find the number of B after 20 days.

1λ = ln2/5.0 = 0.139 day⁻¹. N = 1.0×10²⁰·e^(−0.139×20) = 6.2×10¹⁸ nuclei remain.
2B formed = 1.0×10²⁰ − 6.2×10¹⁸ = 9.38×10¹⁹ nuclei.
EXAMPLE 8 — 80% decays in 4.0 days

Find (i) the decay constant, (ii) the half-life.

1N = 0.2N₀ → 0.2 = e^(−4.0λ) → λ = 0.402 day⁻¹.
2T₁/₂ = ln2/0.402 = 1.72 days.
EXAMPLE 11 — sample activity of ¹⁴C

T₁/₂ = 5730 yr, sample has 1.00×10²² nuclei. Find the activity.

1λ = ln2/T₁/₂ = 3.83×10⁻¹² s⁻¹.
2A = λN = (3.83×10⁻¹²)(1.00×10²²) = 3.84×10¹⁰ decays/s.
12-5 Decay Series & 12-6 Radioactive Dating▶
🟢 In plain words: Sometimes a nucleus decays into something that's also radioactive, which decays again — a "decay chain" that ends at a stable nucleus. We exploit half-lives to date things: living things absorb carbon-14, which decays after death, so the leftover amount reveals an object's age (carbon dating); uranium dates rocks billions of years old.

A decay series occurs when one radioactive isotope decays to another radioactive isotope, which decays again, and so on (e.g. the ²³⁸U series ending at stable ²⁰⁶Pb).

Radioactive dating: the ¹⁴C/¹²C ratio in the atmosphere is roughly constant. A living organism keeps that ratio; when it dies, exchange stops and ¹⁴C (T₁/₂ ≈ 5730 yr) decays away. Measuring the remaining fraction gives the age. Objects older than ~60,000 yr have too little ¹⁴C. ²³⁸U (T₁/₂ = 4.5×10⁹ yr) dates the oldest rocks (~4 billion yr).

EXAMPLE 13 — age of an ancient bone

200 g of carbon registers an activity of 16 decays/s. Find its age. (¹⁴C/¹²C ≈ 1.3×10⁻¹².)

1N(¹²C) in 200 g ≈ 1×10²⁵; N₀(¹⁴C) = 1.3×10⁻¹²·1×10²⁵ = 1.3×10¹³.
2λ = ln2/T₁/₂ = 3.8×10⁻¹² s⁻¹; A₀ = λN₀ = 49.4 decays/s.
3A = A₀e^(−λt), 16 = 49.4·e^(−λt) → t ≈ 9417 years.

Nuclear Reactions

13-1 Nuclear Reactions & Transmutation▶
🟢 In plain words: Fire a particle at a nucleus and you can change one element into another (transmutation — the old alchemists' dream, but real). The energy released or absorbed, Q, comes from the change in total mass (E = mc²). Positive Q = energy out; negative Q = you must supply energy.

A nuclear reaction occurs when a nucleus is struck by another nucleus or particle. If the original nucleus is transformed into another, it is called transmutation — e.g. ⁴₂He + ¹⁴₇N → ¹⁷₈O + ¹₁H. Energy and momentum are conserved; ΣZ and ΣA are conserved.

For a generic reaction a + X → Y + b, the reaction energy Q (released as kinetic energy of products + any gamma photon):

Q = (Δm)c²,   Δm = mᵢ − m_f = (M_a + M_X) − (M_b + M_Y)

Q (or Δm) positive → exothermic (exoergic), energy released. Negative → endothermic (endoergic), energy must be absorbed. Also Q = (K_b + K_Y) − (K_a + K_X). 1 u = 931.5 MeV/c² = 1.66×10⁻²⁷ kg; neutrons are effective (no charge → not repelled).

EXAMPLE 1 — polonium alpha decay

²¹²₈₄Po → ²⁰⁸₈₂Pb + ⁴₂He + Q. (m_Po = 211.98885 u, m_Pb = 207.97664 u, m_α = 4.0026 u.) Find (a) Q in MeV, (b) the gamma wavelength.

1Δm = 211.98885 − (207.97664 + 4.0026) = 9.61×10⁻³ u → Q = 9.61×10⁻³ × 931.5 = 8.95 MeV (≈8.98 via kg method).
2Q = 1.436×10⁻¹² J; λ = hc/Q = (6.63×10⁻³⁴)(3×10⁸)/(1.436×10⁻¹²) = 1.39×10⁻¹³ m.
EXAMPLE 2 — slow neutron reaction

n + ¹⁰₅B → ⁷₃Li + ⁴₂He. With K_n ≈ 0, the He (4.0026 u) moves at 9.30×10⁶ m/s. Find (a) the Li (7.0160 u) kinetic energy, (b) Q.

1Momentum conservation: 0 = m_Li v_Li + m_He v_He → v_Li = −5.323×10⁶ m/s. K_Li = ½m_Li v_Li² = 1.64×10⁻¹³ J = 1.025 MeV.
2K_He = ½m_He v_He² = 1.7958 MeV; Q = (K_Li + K_He) − 0 = 1.03 + 1.8 = 2.83 MeV.
13-2 Nuclear Fission & Reactors▶
🟢 In plain words: Fission = splitting a big heavy nucleus (like uranium) into two smaller ones, releasing huge energy plus spare neutrons. Those neutrons split more nuclei → a chain reaction. A power station controls it (moderators, control rods); a bomb lets it run wild. This is how nuclear power plants work.

Nuclear fission: a heavy nucleus splits into two lighter nuclei of nearly equal mass, emitting neutrons and energy. It releases far more energy than a chemical reaction, because the products' binding energy per nucleon exceeds the parent's. Types: spontaneous (rare) and induced (bombarding with slow/thermal neutrons, ~10⁻² eV).

The emitted neutrons can induce further fission — a chain reaction. A reactor keeps it self-sustaining but controlled: a moderator (heavy water, graphite) slows neutrons; natural uranium (0.7% fissionable) is usually enriched to 2–3%. Below the critical mass too many neutrons escape and the chain stops. An atomic bomb is a deliberate uncontrolled chain reaction.

EXAMPLE 4 — energy from 20 kg of U-235

²³⁵₉₂U + n → ⁸⁹₃₆Kr + ¹⁴⁴₅₆Ba + 3n + Q. (m_U = 235.04393, m_n = 1.00867, m_Kr = 88.91756, m_Ba = 143.92273 u.)

1Δm = (235.04393 + 1.00867) − (88.91756 + 143.92273 + 3×1.00867) = 0.1863 u.
2Q per nucleus = 0.1863 × 931.5 = 174 MeV.
3Nuclei in 20 kg = (20/235×10⁻³)·6.02×10²³ = 5.12×10²⁵; total = 5.12×10²⁵ × 174 = 8.91×10²⁷ MeV.
13-3 Nuclear Fusion▶
🟢 In plain words: Fusion = joining two light nuclei (like hydrogen) into a heavier one, releasing even more energy than fission. It's what powers the Sun and stars. The catch: the nuclei repel fiercely, so you need staggering temperatures (~100 million °C) to force them together — which is why fusion power on Earth is still so hard.

Nuclear fusion: two light nuclei fuse into a heavier nucleus, releasing large energy (thermonuclear energy). Because both nuclei are positive, very high kinetic energies (temperatures ~10⁸ K) are needed to overcome Coulomb repulsion; reactants form a plasma. Fusion occurs in fusion bombs and stellar cores (the Sun fuses H → He: net 4¹₁H → ⁴₂He + 2e⁺ + 2ν + 2γ).

Fission vs fusion: fission splits a heavy nucleus, occurs at controllable temperature, easier to sustain; fusion combines light nuclei, needs ~10⁸ K, hard to sustain. Both produce energy. On the binding-energy-per-nucleon curve, the rising part (low A) favours fusion, the falling part (high A) favours fission; ⁵⁶Fe is the most stable.

EXAMPLE 5 — D-D fusion energy

²₁H + ²₁H → ³₁H + ¹₁H. (m_D = 2.014102, m_T = 3.016049, m_p = 1.007825 u.) Find (a) Q, (b) energy from 1.0 kg deuterium.

1Δm = (2.014102 + 2.014102) − (3.016049 + 1.007825) = 4.33×10⁻³ u → Q = 4.33×10⁻³ × 931.5 = 4.03 MeV.
22 deuterium = 4×10⁻³ kg → 6.02×10²³ nuclei; 1.0 kg → 1.505×10²⁶ nuclei. Energy = 1.505×10²⁶ × 4.03 = 6.07×10²⁶ MeV.
EXAMPLE 6 — estimating fusion energy

²₁H + ²₁H → ⁴₂He. (m_D = 2.014 u, m_He = 4.0026 u.)

1Δm = 2.014 + 2.014 − 4.0026 = 0.0254 u → Q = 0.0254 × 931.5 = ≈ 23.66 MeV.
