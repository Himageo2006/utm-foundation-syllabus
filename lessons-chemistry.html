Thermochemistry

Thermochemistry is the study of energy (heat) changes during chemical reactions. These lessons follow the chapter slides exactly.

1.1 ‚ÄĒ Systems, surroundings & the First Law‚Ė∂
ūüüĘ In plain words: The "system" is just the bit you're studying (the chemicals in the beaker); the "surroundings" is everything else. The First Law is simple bookkeeping: energy is never lost, only moved ‚ÄĒ whatever heat or work leaves the system goes into the surroundings, and vice versa.

The system is the part of the universe we study; the surroundings are everything else. Four types:

  • Open ‚ÄĒ exchanges mass and energy (open beaker).
  • Closed ‚ÄĒ exchanges energy only (coffee-cup calorimeter).
  • Insulated ‚ÄĒ no energy exchange (adiabatic).
  • Isolated ‚ÄĒ no mass or energy exchange (thermoflask).

First Law of Thermodynamics

Energy cannot be created or destroyed: E(universe) = E(system) + E(surroundings) = 0.

őĒE = q + w   (q = heat, w = work = PőĒV)

Sign convention: +q heat enters the system; ‚ąíq heat leaves. +w work done on the system; ‚ąíw work done by the system.

1.2 ‚ÄĒ Enthalpy, exothermic & endothermic‚Ė∂
ūüüĘ In plain words: "Enthalpy" is just a fancy word for the heat stored in chemicals. őĒH is how much that changes during a reaction. Exothermic = gives out heat (gets hot, őĒH negative) ‚ÄĒ like a fire. Endothermic = soaks up heat (gets cold, őĒH positive) ‚ÄĒ like an instant cold pack.

Enthalpy (H) is the heat content at constant pressure. We measure the change, őĒH:

őĒH = őĒU + PőĒV  ¬∑  őĒH(reaction) = H(products) ‚ąí H(reactants)

At constant pressure őĒH = qp; at constant volume őĒH = qv.

Two types

  • Exothermic ‚ÄĒ releases heat, surroundings warm up (Tfinal > Tinitial), őĒH negative. Heat is a product.
  • Endothermic ‚ÄĒ absorbs heat, surroundings cool, őĒH positive. Heat is a reactant.

Example (exothermic): H‚āā(g) + ¬ĹO‚āā(g) ‚Üí H‚āāO(l), őĒH = ‚ąíq kJ.

The formula q = m √ó c √ó őĒT turns that temperature change into joules. A "coffee-cup" calorimeter does it at constant pressure; a sealed "bomb" does it at constant volume for burning things.

A calorimeter measures heats of reaction. Two types: the solution (coffee-cup) calorimeter (constant pressure) and the bomb calorimeter (constant volume, for combustion).

1.4 ‚ÄĒ Standard enthalpies (formation, combustion, neutralisation, solution)‚Ė∂

Chemical Kinetics

Kinetics is the study of how fast reactions go ‚ÄĒ the change in concentration of a reactant or product with time (M/s). Built directly from the chapter slides.

2.1 ‚ÄĒ Rate & the differential rate equation‚Ė∂
ūüüĘ In plain words: "Rate" just means how fast the reaction goes ‚ÄĒ how quickly reactants disappear or products appear, per second. Reactants count as going down, products as going up. The balancing numbers in the equation tell you the speed ratios (if one is used up twice as fast, that's the "2" in front).
Rate = (change in concentration) / (time taken)   (always positive, M¬∑s‚ĀĽ¬Ļ)

Reactant concentration decreases (‚ąí); product increases (+). For aA + bB ‚Üí cC + dD the rates link via the coefficients:

2.5 ‚ÄĒ Collision theory, activation energy & factors‚Ė∂
ūüüĘ In plain words: Reactions happen when molecules bump into each other hard enough and at the right angle. The minimum "hard enough" energy is the activation energy. Anything that makes useful collisions more frequent ‚ÄĒ more concentration, higher temperature, bigger surface area, or a catalyst ‚ÄĒ speeds things up.

Collision theory: molecules react only if they collide with (1) enough energy (‚Č• activation energy E‚āź) and (2) the correct orientation.

Activation energy (E‚āź) = minimum collision energy needed to break bonds and start the reaction. Lower E‚āź ‚Üí faster reaction. The transition state (activated complex) is the unstable high-energy peak with partially broken/formed bonds.

Factors that increase rate

  • Concentration ‚ÜĎ ‚ÄĒ more collisions.
  • Temperature ‚ÜĎ ‚ÄĒ more frequent & more energetic collisions.
  • Surface area of solids ‚ÜĎ ‚ÄĒ more contact.
  • Catalyst ‚ÄĒ lowers E‚āź.
2.6 ‚ÄĒ Reaction mechanism & rate-determining step‚Ė∂
ūüüĘ In plain words: Most reactions happen in several small steps, not all at once. The slowest step is the bottleneck ‚ÄĒ it sets the overall speed, just like the slowest checkout decides how fast a queue moves. An "intermediate" is something made in one step and used up in a later one, so it never appears in the overall equation.

A mechanism is a series of elementary steps that sum to the overall equation. Key terms:

  • Rate-determining step ‚ÄĒ the slowest step; it sets the rate law.
  • Intermediate ‚ÄĒ appears in a step but not the overall equation.
  • Molecularity ‚ÄĒ number of molecules in a step (unimolecular, bimolecular, termolecular).

2.7 ‚ÄĒ Catalysts‚Ė∂
ūüüĘ In plain words: A catalyst is a helper that speeds up a reaction without being used up ‚ÄĒ it offers an easier path with a lower energy hurdle, then comes out unchanged at the end, ready to help again. The catalytic converter in a car is a famous example.

A catalyst speeds up a reaction by lowering E‚āź (providing an alternative pathway) and is not consumed. Two types:

  • Homogeneous ‚ÄĒ same phase as reactants (e.g. Br‚āā catalysing H‚āāO‚āā decomposition; forms an intermediate).
  • Heterogeneous ‚ÄĒ different phase (e.g. platinum in a car catalytic converter: adsorption ‚Üí reaction ‚Üí desorption).

Chemical Equilibrium

Equilibrium applies to the extent of a reaction ‚ÄĒ how far it goes once no further change occurs. Built directly from the chapter slides.

3.1 ‚ÄĒ Dynamic equilibrium & reversibility‚Ė∂
ūüüĘ In plain words: Some reactions can run both forwards and backwards at the same time. "Equilibrium" is when both directions go at the same speed, so the amounts stop changing ‚ÄĒ but it's not stopped, it's a busy balance (like walking up a downward escalator and staying in one spot). That's why we call it dynamic.

Irreversible reactions go in one direction almost exclusively (go to completion), e.g. 2Mg(s) + O‚āā(g) ‚Üí 2MgO(s). Reversible reactions go both ways at comparable rates, e.g. CaCO‚āÉ(s) ‚áĆ CaO(s) + CO‚āā(g) and N‚āā(g) + 3H‚āā(g) ‚áĆ 2NH‚āÉ(g).

In a closed vessel the forward (k‚āĀ) and reverse (k‚āč‚āĀ) reactions continue at the same rate ‚ÄĒ the system looks "finished" but is dynamic:

At equilibrium: rateforward = ratereverse
On a concentration-vs-time graph, reactants fall and products rise until both level off ‚ÄĒ that plateau is equilibrium.
3.2 ‚ÄĒ The equilibrium constant expression (Kc & Kp)‚Ė∂
ūüüĘ In plain words: K is a single number that tells you the balance point of a reaction: products multiplied together, divided by reactants (each raised to its balancing number). Big K = mostly products; small K = mostly reactants. Use Kc when you measure concentrations, Kp when you measure gas pressures.

For the equilibrium aA + bB ‚áĆ cC + dD:

K = ([C]Š∂ú[D]ŠĶą) / ([A]ŠĶÉ[B]ŠĶá)   (K depends only on temperature)

Kc uses concentrations; Kp uses partial pressures.

3.4 ‚ÄĒ Homogeneous vs heterogeneous equilibria‚Ė∂
ūüüĘ In plain words: If everything is in the same state (all gases, say) it's "homogeneous"; mixed states is "heterogeneous." The key rule to remember: pure solids and pure liquids are left out of the K expression (their "concentration" doesn't change), so only gases and dissolved things appear.

Homogeneous = all same state; heterogeneous = different states. Solids (s) and liquids (l) have constant concentration and are excluded from K. Only (g) and (aq) appear.

3.9 ‚ÄĒ Le Chatelier's principle & the four factors‚Ė∂
ūüüĘ In plain words: Le Chatelier's principle = "poke a balanced system and it pushes back." 3.11 ‚ÄĒ The common ion effect‚Ė∂
ūüüĘ In plain words: If a solid is dissolving and you add more of one of its own ions from another source, the dissolving stalls ‚ÄĒ less of the solid dissolves than before. Acids & Bases

Acid‚Äďbase equilibrium is the equilibrium of ions in aqueous solution (water as solvent). Built directly from the chapter slides.

4.1 ‚ÄĒ Three definitions: Arrhenius, Br√łnsted‚ÄďLowry, Lewis‚Ė∂
ūüüĘ In plain words: There are three ways to define an acid/base, each wider than the last. Arrhenius: acids make H‚Āļ, bases make OH‚ĀĽ. Br√łnsted‚ÄďLowry: acids give away a proton (H‚Āļ), bases take one. Lewis (widest): it's all about electron pairs ‚ÄĒ bases donate them, acids accept them. Same chemicals, just zoomed-out viewpoints.
  • Arrhenius: acid generates H‚Āļ (HCl ‚Üí H‚Āļ + Cl‚ĀĽ); base generates OH‚ĀĽ (NaOH ‚Üí Na‚Āļ + OH‚ĀĽ). Limited to H/OH compounds in water only ‚ÄĒ can't explain NH‚āÉ or CO‚āā.
  • Br√łnsted‚ÄďLowry: acid donates a proton; base accepts one. Works for reversible reactions and forms conjugate pairs differing by one H‚Āļ.
  • Lewis (widest): acid = electron-pair acceptor (electrophile, e.g. BF‚āÉ, Al¬≥‚Āļ); base = electron-pair donor (nucleophile, e.g. NH‚āÉ, OH‚ĀĽ).

Classifying acids by protons: monoprotic (HCl‚ÜíH‚Āļ), diprotic (H‚āāSO‚āĄ‚Üí2H‚Āļ), triprotic (H‚āÉPO‚āĄ‚Üí3H‚Āļ).

4.2 ‚ÄĒ Kw, pH & the pH scale‚Ė∂
ūüüĘ In plain words: pH is just a handy number for "how acidic" something is, from 0 (very acidic) to 14 (very basic), with 7 neutral. It's really a measure of how much H‚Āļ is floating around: pH = ‚ąílog[H‚Āļ]. Because acid and base levels always multiply to a fixed value (Kw), pH + pOH = 14.

Autoionization of water: 2H‚āāO ‚áĆ H‚āÉO‚Āļ + OH‚ĀĽ. Since [H‚āāO] is constant:

4.3 ‚ÄĒ Strength: strong vs weak; Ka, Kb & % ionization‚Ė∂
ūüüĘ In plain words: "Strong" doesn't mean concentrated ‚ÄĒ it means the acid/base fully splits apart in water (100%). "Weak" ones only partly split. Ka and Kb measure that splitting: bigger value = stronger. There are just 7 common strong acids and 7 strong bases worth memorising; assume everything else is weak.

Strong acids/bases ionize 100% (single arrow). 7 strong acids: HCl, HBr, HI, H‚āāSO‚āĄ, HNO‚āÉ, HClO‚āÉ, HClO‚āĄ. 7 strong bases: LiOH, NaOH, KOH, RbOH, Ca(OH)‚āā, Sr(OH)‚āā, Ba(OH)‚āā.

Organic Chemistry

The chemistry of carbon compounds (except CO, CO‚āā, carbonates, carbides). Over 7 million known; mostly molecular, built from six non-metals C, H, O, N, S, P. Built directly from the chapter slides.

6.1 ‚ÄĒ Classification & carbon bonding‚Ė∂
ūüüĘ In plain words: Organic chemistry is the chemistry of carbon. Carbon is special because it always makes exactly 4 bonds and links to itself in long chains and rings ‚ÄĒ that's why there are millions of carbon compounds. "Hydrocarbons" are the simplest: just carbon and hydrogen.

Hydrocarbons contain only C and H. Saturated = single bonds only; unsaturated = multiple bonds. Three families:

  • Aliphatic: alkanes, alkenes, alkynes
  • Cyclic (rings) and Aromatic (benzene/arene)

Carbon forms four bonds: 4 single ‚Üí tetrahedral (sp¬≥); 1 triple+1 single or 2 double ‚Üí linear (sp); 2 single+1 double ‚Üí trigonal planar (sp¬≤).

Solubility depends on the attached groups: CH‚āÉOH is miscible with water, C‚āĀ‚āÄH‚āā‚āĀOH is insoluble (too large). An "‚ÄďOH" generally aids water solubility.

6.3 ‚ÄĒ Formulas & classifying C/H atoms‚Ė∂
ūüüĘ In plain words: There are several ways to draw the same molecule ‚ÄĒ fully spelt out, condensed, or as quick "skeletal" lines (chemists' shorthand where the corners are carbons). Also, carbons get labelled by how many other carbons they touch: primary (1), secondary (2), tertiary (3), quaternary (4).

Ways to depict molecules: molecular (C‚āÖH‚āĀ‚āā), expanded/structural (every bond), condensed (CH‚āÉCH‚āāCH‚āāCH‚āāCH‚āÉ), skeletal (lines, H on C omitted), and 3D (wedge = out of plane, dash = behind, line = in plane).

Carbon atoms: primary (1 alkyl group), secondary (2), tertiary (3), quaternary (4). Hydrogen atoms are 1¬į/2¬į/3¬į by the carbon they sit on.

6.4 ‚ÄĒ Isomerism‚Ė∂
ūüüĘ In plain words: Isomers are molecules with the same ingredients but arranged differently ‚ÄĒ like the same Lego bricks built into different shapes. Same formula, different molecule (and often different properties). They split into structural isomers (different connections) and stereoisomers (same connections, different 3-D layout: cis/trans and mirror-image "chiral" pairs).

Same molecular formula, different arrangement. Two main types:

  • Structural (constitutional): chain isomers (e.g. butane vs isobutane), positional (1- vs 2-bromobutane), functional (propanal vs propanone).
  • Stereoisomerism: geometric cis/trans (needs C=C or a ring ‚ÄĒ rotation locked by the ŌÄ bond) and optical (needs a chiral carbon: 4 different groups ‚Üí non-superimposable mirror images = enantiomers).

Number of structural isomers grows fast: C‚āĄH‚āĀ‚āÄ‚Üí2, C‚āÖH‚āĀ‚āā‚Üí3, C‚āÜH‚āĀ‚āĄ‚Üí5, C‚āáH‚āĀ‚āÜ‚Üí9. (Rotation about a single bond is not isomerism.)

6.5 ‚ÄĒ Naming alkanes (5-step IUPAC)‚Ė∂
ūüüĘ In plain words: Naming follows a recipe: find the longest carbon chain (that's the surname), number it from the end that gives the branches the smallest numbers, then list the branches alphabetically in front. Build it as one word: prefix‚Äďparent‚Äďsuffix.

Name = Prefix‚ÄďParent‚ÄďSuffix (substituents ‚Äď chain length ‚Äď functional group).

1Find the longest continuous chain (= parent). Ties ‚Üí pick the one with more branches.
2Number from the end nearer the first branch (lowest locants).
3Identify & number substituents (methyl, ethyl, ‚Ä¶; halo = chloro/bromo).
4Write as one word, substituents alphabetical, di/tri prefixes for repeats.

6.6 ‚ÄĒ Alkenes & alkynes (naming + cis/trans)‚Ė∂
ūüüĘ In plain words: Alkenes have a double bond (C=C, ending -ene), alkynes a triple bond (C‚Č°C, ending -yne). Naming is the same as alkanes, but the chain must include the double/triple bond and you number from the end nearest it. Because a double bond can't twist, you also get cis (same side) vs trans (opposite sides).

Alkenes (C=C, C‚āôH‚āā‚āô): longest chain must contain the double bond; number from the end nearer C=C; locant before the parent. Alkynes (C‚Č°C, C‚āôH‚āā‚āô‚āč‚āā): same rules, suffix -yne.

6.7 ‚ÄĒ Aromatic compounds (benzene)‚Ė∂
ūüüĘ In plain words: Benzene is a famous 6-carbon ring. Its bonds are "shared out" evenly around the ring, which makes it unusually stable. When you stick groups onto it, you describe their positions as ortho (next to each other, 1,2), meta (1,3) and para (opposite, 1,4).

Benzene C‚āÜH‚āÜ ‚ÄĒ a ring that looks like it has C=C but doesn't behave so (delocalised). Monosubstituted derivatives: methylbenzene (toluene), phenol (‚ÄďOH), aniline (‚ÄďNH‚āā), benzoic acid (‚ÄďCOOH), benzaldehyde, nitrobenzene, bromobenzene, styrene.

As a substituent, benzene = phenyl (e.g. 2-phenyl-4-octene). Disubstituted: ortho (1,2), meta (1,3/1,5), para (1,4) ‚ÄĒ e.g. o-/m-/p-xylene; or number lowest-locant alphabetically (1-bromo-2-chlorobenzene).

6.8 ‚ÄĒ Oxygen & nitrogen families (naming)‚Ė∂
ūüüĘ In plain words: Each family that contains oxygen or nitrogen (alcohols, aldehydes, ketones, acids, esters, amines‚Ä¶) has its own ending and naming rule. The key skill is recognising the functional group, giving it the lowest possible number, and using the right suffix. This table-style lesson is your naming cheat-sheet for all of them.
  • Alkyl halides (R‚ÄďX): name halogen as substituent, e.g. 2-bromopropane, 3-chlorohexane. Classed 1¬į/2¬į/3¬į like the carbon.
  • Alcohols (R‚ÄďOH, suffix -ol): number from end nearer OH; e.g. 2-methyl-2-pentanol. Classed 1¬į/2¬į/3¬į.
  • Aldehydes (-al, C=O always on C1) & ketones (-one, locant given): e.g. 4-methylpentanal, 2-methyl-3-pentanone.
  • Carboxylic acids (-oic acid, C is C1, highest precedence): e.g. 3-chloro-4-ethylhexanoic acid. Made by oxidising 1¬į alcohols/aldehydes.
  • Esters (R‚ÄďCOO‚ÄďR‚Ä≤, -oate): alkyl group first, then chain-oate, e.g. butyl propanoate. From acid + alcohol with conc. H‚āāSO‚āĄ.
  • Ethers (R‚ÄďO‚ÄďR‚Ä≤): smaller group = alkoxy prefix, e.g. methoxyethane (common: ethyl methyl ether).
  • Amines (R‚ÄďNH‚āā, -amine, 1¬į/2¬į/3¬į): e.g. N-methylethanamine. Amides (R‚ÄďCO‚ÄďNH‚āā, -amide): e.g. ethanamide.
6.9 ‚ÄĒ Organic reactions & mechanisms‚Ė∂
ūüüĘ In plain words: A "mechanism" is the step-by-step story of how a reaction happens ‚ÄĒ which bonds break and how. Two key cast members: nucleophiles ("nucleus-lovers," electron-rich, attack positive spots) and electrophiles ("electron-lovers," electron-poor, attack negative spots). The main plot types are addition, elimination, and substitution.

Bond fission: homolytic (split evenly ‚Üí free radicals, fish-hook arrows) vs heterolytic (uneven ‚Üí carbocation/carbanion, full arrows).

Reactive species: nucleophile (Nu‚ĀĽ, electron-pair donor, Lewis base ‚ÄĒ CN‚ĀĽ, OH‚ĀĽ, NH‚āÉ) attacks electron-poor C; electrophile (E‚Āļ, electron-pair acceptor, Lewis acid ‚ÄĒ H‚Āļ, AlCl‚āÉ, Br‚āā) attacks electron-rich sites.

Reaction types: addition (electrophilic to C=C, nucleophilic to C=O), elimination (one molecule ‚Üí two), substitution (nucleophilic ‚ÄĒ swap a group), rearrangement.

6.11 ‚ÄĒ Polymers (macromolecules)‚Ė∂
ūüüĘ In plain words: A polymer is a giant molecule made by joining loads of small identical units (monomers) into a long chain ‚ÄĒ like threading many identical beads into a necklace. Plastics, nylon, DNA and proteins are all polymers. They form two ways: addition (units with a C=C just click together) or condensation (units join and spit out a small molecule like water).

A polymer = large molecule built from many repeating monomers ("poly" = many, "mono" = one). Natural: rubber, proteins, cellulose, DNA. Synthetic: PE, PVC, PP, nylon, polyester.

Chain structures (increasing strength): linear ‚Üí branched ‚Üí cross-linked ‚Üí network. Sequence: homopolymer (‚ÄďA-A-A‚Äď) vs copolymer (alternating, block, graft, random).

Two polymerizations:

  • Addition (chain): monomers with C=C add up, no by-product ‚ÄĒ initiation/propagation/termination. e.g. ethene ‚Üí polyethylene, styrene ‚Üí polystyrene.
  • Condensation (stepwise): monomers with two reactive ends join, eliminating a small molecule (H‚āāO). e.g. nylon 6,6 (amide links), polyester (ester links).

States: amorphous (glass transition Tg) vs crystalline (melting Tm). Types: fibres (strong forces), plastics (thermoplastics reshape on heating / thermosets harden), elastomers (weak forces, cross-linked).
